## 1.1 Background of the Study

The emergence of electronic commerce (e-commerce) has fundamentally reshaped the global retail landscape, altering the way consumers search for, evaluate, and purchase goods and services. Since the early commercialisation of the World Wide Web in the 1990s, online shopping has grown from a niche technology-driven activity into a mainstream channel that accounts for trillions of dollars in global transactions annually. Michael Aldrich, an English innovator, is widely credited with inventing online shopping in 1979 by connecting a modified television set to a real-time transaction processing computer via a domestic telephone line, enabling the first electronic consumer transactions (Vaidya, 2019). The subsequent introduction of the World Wide Web by Tim Berners-Lee in 1990, and the launch of Amazon's book retail service in 1995, set the foundation for the modern e-commerce economy that now encompasses virtually every product and service category imaginable.

In developing economies, e-commerce adoption has followed a different trajectory compared to developed nations, often shaped by factors such as internet penetration rates, smartphone ownership, digital payment infrastructure, regulatory frameworks, and consumer trust. Nepal is a prime example of such a developing-economy context. The history of organised retail in Nepal dates back almost a century, but the country's online shopping journey is considerably more recent. The first e-commerce activity in Nepal can be traced to 1999, when internet services were used primarily by Nepalese citizens residing abroad to send gifts to family members at home (Devkota et al., 2021). The formal launch of muncha.com around 2000 marked Nepal's first serious attempt at an online retail platform, and the ensuing two decades have seen the sector evolve rapidly (Vaidya, 2019). Today, platforms such as Daraz—which was acquired by Alibaba Group in 2018—Sasto Deal, Hamrobazar, and a proliferating ecosystem of social media–based sellers collectively constitute Nepal's growing e-commerce market.

Several macro-level drivers have accelerated this growth. Internet penetration in Nepal increased dramatically from less than 30 percent of the population in 2010 to approximately 63 percent by late 2017 (Devkota et al., 2021). The proliferation of affordable 3G and 4G smartphones, combined with competitive mobile data pricing, has brought internet access to a broad cross-section of the population, including urban youth, working professionals, and increasingly, semi-urban and rural households. The COVID-19 pandemic provided a further, decisive impetus: with physical retail suspended during lockdown periods, many Nepalese consumers turned to online platforms for the first time, and the habit has persisted post-pandemic (Pandey, 2025). Statista projects that online transaction value in Nepal will grow at an annual rate of 9.62 percent, reaching USD 3.35 billion by 2028 (Pandey, 2025), underscoring the sector's long-term strategic importance.

Despite these growth indicators, the Nepalese e-commerce sector is characterised by a set of persistent structural and experiential challenges that have not been adequately addressed in the academic literature. Three interrelated challenges stand out as particularly consequential for consumers: payment problems, online scams, and product quality mismatch. Payment-related difficulties remain a stubborn barrier. Nepal's digital payment infrastructure, while improving, still suffers from unreliable gateways, frequent transaction failures, limited payment options, slow refund processes, and the prevalence of hidden charges (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021). Many consumers continue to prefer Cash on Delivery (COD) as a default payment method precisely because of their distrust of electronic payment systems (Pandey, 2025). Online scams have proliferated alongside the sector's growth: the Nepal Cyber Bureau reported more than 2,000 cases of online marketplace fraud in 2025, involving losses exceeding NPR 31 million, including incidents of non-delivery, counterfeit products, fraudulent payment gateways, and phishing attacks (Shrestha et al., 2025). Product quality mismatch is perhaps the most consistently cited complaint among Nepalese online shoppers. Vaidya (2019) found that the quality of delivered products was the single largest problem in Nepalese online shopping, while Devkota et al. (2021) reported that 41.6 percent of respondents in their survey of Kathmandu Valley residents identified products not matching what was shown online as the most common challenge.

These three dimensions of consumer experience—payment problems, scam exposure, and product quality mismatch—do not operate in isolation. Each has the potential to elevate consumers' subjective sense of risk associated with online shopping. Risk perception, broadly understood as the consumer's subjective assessment of the likelihood and severity of an adverse outcome in an online transaction (Pavlou, 2003), has been identified in a substantial body of international literature as a key mediating variable linking online shopping problems to consumer behavioural outcomes. Kim et al. (2008) demonstrated that perceived risk exerts a strong negative influence on purchase intentions in electronic commerce, while Hipólito et al. (2025) confirmed that risk perception significantly shapes consumer satisfaction with the online shopping experience. Bianchi and Andrews (2012) showed that in developing-country contexts, perceived risk has a particularly pronounced inverse relationship with consumers' attitudes and purchase intentions. Pandey (2025) provided direct empirical evidence from Nepal, establishing that financial risk—stemming from unreliable payment systems and security concerns—significantly and negatively affects online buying behaviour among Nepalese consumers.

Risk perception in the e-commerce context is a multi-dimensional construct that encompasses several distinct but interrelated components. Financial risk refers to the potential for monetary loss arising from non-delivery, overcharging, or payment fraud. Product risk pertains to the possibility that the product received will differ from the product ordered in terms of quality, specifications, or condition. Privacy risk concerns the potential for personal or financial information to be intercepted, misused, or disclosed without the consumer's consent. Performance risk relates to the possibility that the product or service will not function as expected. Finally, psychological risk reflects the anxiety and discomfort that consumers experience when engaging in uncertain transactions (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008). In the Nepalese context, all of these risk dimensions are activated by the three problem categories under study: payment failures create financial and privacy risk; scams create financial, privacy, and psychological risk; and product quality mismatches create product and performance risk. Understanding how these risks interact and aggregate to shape post-purchase behaviour is therefore a theoretically important and practically consequential undertaking.

The concept of post-purchase behaviour occupies a central position in consumer behaviour theory. Classical consumer decision-making models—such as the Engel-Blackwell-Miniard (EBM) model—identify post-purchase evaluation as the final stage of the purchase process, in which consumers compare the actual performance of a product or service against their pre-purchase expectations. When performance meets or exceeds expectations, consumers experience satisfaction, which reinforces positive attitudes toward the seller or platform and increases the likelihood of repeat purchase and positive word-of-mouth. When performance falls short of expectations—as is frequently the case in Nepalese online shopping due to the three problems identified above—consumers experience cognitive dissonance and dissatisfaction, which can manifest in a variety of post-purchase behaviours including formal complaints, product returns, negative online reviews, word-of-mouth warnings to friends and family, and the decision to switch platforms or abandon online shopping altogether (Tanjung et al., 2025; Izumi et al., 2025; Ghimire, 2023).

Post-purchase behaviour, the set of actions, evaluations, and responses that occur after a transaction is completed, is the downstream outcome of consumer experience and risk perception. Tanjung et al. (2025) identified a spectrum of post-purchase complaint behaviours ranging from direct complaint to sellers (voicing), to angry word-of-mouth (irate responses), to silent disengagement (passive behaviour), while Izumi et al. (2025) found that product quality failures and wrong deliveries were the most frequent triggers for product returns and negative reviews. Ghimire (2023) noted that prior negative online shopping experiences substantially shape the decision-making processes of Nepalese online shoppers in subsequent transactions, creating a feedback loop between post-purchase dissatisfaction and future consumer behaviour.

In spite of the growing volume of online shopping activity in Nepal, the academic literature addressing the interplay of payment issues, scams, product quality mismatch, risk perception, and post-purchase behaviour in the Nepalese context remains fragmented and limited. Existing studies have tended to examine these dimensions in isolation, or to examine consumer behaviour in online shopping more broadly without a specifically post-purchase focus. The present study seeks to address this gap by developing and testing an integrated model in which payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch function as independent variables that influence post-purchase behaviour, with risk perception as a mediating variable. By situating this model within the specific socio-economic and technological context of Nepal, the study aims to make both theoretical and practical contributions to the emerging literature on e-commerce consumer behaviour in South Asian developing economies.

## 1.2 Statement of the Problem

The rapid growth of online shopping in Nepal has generated both opportunity and challenge. While the convenience and accessibility of e-commerce platforms have attracted a growing number of Nepalese consumers, the quality of the online shopping experience has not kept pace with the sector's quantitative expansion. Consumers continue to encounter significant problems at multiple stages of the online purchase process, and these problems are shaping consumer risk perceptions and post-purchase behaviour in ways that have important implications for the long-term sustainability of Nepal's e-commerce market.

Three problem areas in particular demand academic and practitioner attention. First, payment-related difficulties persist as a structural weakness of the Nepalese e-commerce ecosystem. Payment failures, gateway unreliability, limited digital payment options, slow or absent refund mechanisms, and the imposition of hidden charges undermine consumer confidence in the financial safety of online transactions (Devkota et al., 2021; Pandey, 2025). These payment problems are compounded by weaknesses in Nepal's broader digital financial infrastructure and by the limited financial and technological literacy of a significant portion of the consumer population (Chapagain & Aryal, 2026). The prevalence of COD as a consumer preference is, in itself, a symptom of the underlying distrust of digital payment channels.

Second, online fraud and scams have become a systemic concern in Nepal's e-commerce sector. Reports from the Nepal Cyber Bureau document thousands of cases annually involving fake sellers, non-delivery of goods, counterfeit products, fraudulent payment gateways, and phishing attacks (Shrestha et al., 2025). The damage from such incidents extends well beyond direct financial losses: exposure to scams or even awareness of their prevalence erodes consumer trust in online platforms more broadly, reducing purchase intentions and increasing the perceived riskiness of online transactions (Kim et al., 2008; Nguyen et al., 2025). This trust erosion is particularly difficult to reverse because it operates at a psychological and reputational level that is not easily addressed by technical fixes alone.

Third, product quality mismatch—the discrepancy between the product as presented on an online platform and the product as actually received—is a pervasive source of consumer dissatisfaction in the Nepalese context. Vaidya (2019) identified this as the leading problem in Nepalese online shopping, while Devkota et al. (2021) reported that more than 40 percent of surveyed consumers had experienced products that did not match online descriptions. Product mismatches, damaged deliveries, misleading product descriptions, and the difficulty of returning unsatisfactory products collectively degrade the post-purchase experience and generate dissatisfaction, complaints, and platform-switching behaviour (Tanjung et al., 2025; Hipólito et al., 2025).

The central problem addressed by this study is the lack of an integrated, empirically tested understanding of how these three categories of online shopping problems—payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch—jointly and individually affect consumers' risk perception and, through that perception, their post-purchase behaviour and satisfaction. The existing Nepalese literature tends to treat these problems as isolated concerns rather than as components of an interconnected system of risk that shapes the overall consumer experience. Furthermore, the mediating role of risk perception in translating pre- and during-purchase problems into post-purchase behavioural outcomes has not been examined in a single, unified empirical study in the Nepalese context.

Academically, the absence of such an integrated study constitutes a significant gap in the Nepalese e-commerce literature. Vaidya (2019) documented the preferences and problems of Nepalese online shoppers without modelling the mechanisms linking those problems to risk perception or post-purchase outcomes. Devkota et al. (2021) described consumer perceptions of online shopping challenges but did not empirically test the relationships between challenges, risk, and behavioural outcomes. Pandey (2025) focused specifically on financial and product risk as determinants of buying behaviour but did not examine the full spectrum of post-purchase responses or include scams and payment failures as distinct independent constructs. Shrestha et al. (2025) examined the effects of scam exposure on trust but did not extend the analysis to post-purchase behaviour or incorporate payment issues and product quality mismatch within the same model. This fragmentation of the literature means that no single study has provided a comprehensive, empirically validated account of how the three most salient problem categories in Nepalese online shopping collectively shape consumer risk perception and post-purchase behaviour.

This study therefore asks: To what extent do payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch influence post-purchase behaviour among online shoppers in Nepal, and does risk perception mediate these relationships? Answering this question will help fill a significant gap in the academic literature on e-commerce consumer behaviour in developing economies and will provide evidence-based guidance for platform operators, policymakers, and consumer protection bodies in Nepal.

## 1.3 Research Objectives

### 1.3.1 General Objective

The general objective of this study is to examine the influence of payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch on the post-purchase behaviour of online shoppers in Nepal, with risk perception as a mediating variable, and to determine the relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction in the context of Nepalese e-commerce.

### 1.3.2 Specific Objectives

The specific objectives of this study are as follows:

1. To identify the common payment problems faced by online shoppers in Nepal and examine their influence on consumer risk perception.

2. To study the nature and prevalence of online scams in Nepal's e-commerce sector and assess their effect on consumer trust and post-purchase behaviour.

3. To understand the extent of product quality mismatch experienced by online shoppers in Nepal and its impact on consumer dissatisfaction and complaint behaviour.

4. To examine how payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch collectively and individually influence consumers' post-purchase behaviour in the Nepalese online shopping context.

5. To investigate the mediating role of risk perception in the relationship between online shopping problems (payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch) and post-purchase behaviour.

6. To determine the relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction in Nepal's online shopping environment.

---

## 1.4 Research Questions

The following research questions guide the investigation:

1. What types of payment problems are most commonly experienced by online shoppers in Nepal, and how do these problems affect their perception of online shopping risk?

2. How do the prevalence of and exposure to online scams affect consumer trust and post-purchase behaviour among Nepalese online shoppers?

3. To what extent does product quality mismatch contribute to consumer dissatisfaction and influence post-purchase behaviour in Nepal's e-commerce market?

4. What is the combined and individual influence of payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch on the post-purchase behaviour of Nepalese online shoppers?

5. Does risk perception mediate the relationship between online shopping problems (payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch) and post-purchase behaviour in the Nepalese context?

6. What is the nature and strength of the relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction in Nepal's online shopping environment?

---

## 1.5 Research Hypotheses

Based on the review of existing literature and the theoretical framework, the following hypotheses are proposed for empirical testing:

**H1:** Payment issues have a significant positive effect on risk perception among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H2:** Online scams have a significant positive effect on risk perception among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H3:** Product quality mismatch has a significant positive effect on risk perception among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H4:** Risk perception has a significant positive effect on post-purchase behaviour (complaint, return, negative word-of-mouth, and platform-switching) among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H5:** Risk perception mediates the relationship between payment issues and post-purchase behaviour among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H6:** Risk perception mediates the relationship between online scams and post-purchase behaviour among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H7:** Risk perception mediates the relationship between product quality mismatch and post-purchase behaviour among online shoppers in Nepal.

**H8:** Risk perception has a significant negative effect on customer satisfaction among online shoppers in Nepal.

These hypotheses are grounded in established theoretical propositions. The positive relationship between online shopping problems and risk perception is consistent with Pavlou's (2003) finding that monetary loss risk, privacy risk, and informational uncertainty—all forms of risk that payment failures, scams, and product mismatches engender—are key determinants of e-commerce acceptance. Kim et al. (2008) provided structural equation modelling evidence that perceived risk exerts a strong negative influence on e-commerce purchasing intentions, while Hipólito et al. (2025) confirmed that risk perception negatively affects online shopping satisfaction. The hypothesised mediating role of risk perception aligns with Bianchi and Andrews' (2012) model in which perceived risk translates online shopping problems into attitudinal and behavioural outcomes, and with Pandey's (2025) empirical findings from Nepal establishing the direct negative influence of financial and product risk on online buying behaviour.

The specific hypothesis that scams exert a positive effect on risk perception is consistent with the findings of Shrestha et al. (2025), who demonstrated that consumer awareness of fraudulent incidents significantly shapes trust and preference in Nepal's e-commerce sector, and with Nguyen et al. (2025), who showed that cyber-fraud perception influences both perceived behavioural control and purchase behaviour on e-commerce platforms. The hypothesis linking product quality mismatch to risk perception is supported by the work of Tanjung et al. (2025), who established that perceived product quality failures are primary antecedents of dissatisfaction and complaint behaviour in e-commerce, and by Devkota et al.'s (2021) documentation of the prevalence of product mismatches in the Nepalese context. The hypothesis concerning the negative relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction is consistent with a convergent body of evidence from both developed and developing-country contexts (Kim et al., 2008; Bianchi & Andrews, 2012; Hipólito et al., 2025; Pandey, 2025).

## 1.6 Significance of the Study

This study makes significant contributions at multiple levels: academic, managerial, and policy.

### 1.6.1 Academic Significance

The study contributes to the growing body of knowledge on e-commerce consumer behaviour in developing economies, particularly in the South Asian context. While a substantial theoretical and empirical literature exists on risk perception and online shopping behaviour in developed-country settings (Kim et al., 2008; Pavlou, 2003; Hipólito et al., 2025), studies that integrate multiple dimensions of online shopping problems—payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch—within a single mediational framework are relatively scarce, especially in the Nepalese context. The present study extends the theoretical models of Pavlou (2003) and Kim et al. (2008) by applying them to the specific socio-economic conditions of a small, developing economy with a nascent e-commerce ecosystem, weak regulatory oversight, and a consumer population with diverse levels of digital literacy. The inclusion of risk perception as a mediator, rather than merely a moderator or direct predictor, provides a more nuanced theoretical account of the mechanisms through which online shopping problems translate into post-purchase outcomes.

Furthermore, by integrating three distinct categories of online shopping problem—payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch—within a single empirical model, the study addresses a fragmentation in the existing Nepalese e-commerce literature, where these issues have typically been studied in isolation (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021; Shrestha et al., 2025; Pandey, 2025). The findings will offer a more holistic understanding of the consumer experience in Nepalese online shopping and contribute to the theoretical development of integrated frameworks for e-commerce risk and post-purchase behaviour research.

### 1.6.2 Managerial Significance

For e-commerce platform operators and online retailers in Nepal, the findings of this study will offer evidence-based guidance on the specific problem areas that most significantly affect consumer risk perception and post-purchase behaviour. Understanding which of the three problem categories—payment issues, scams, or product quality mismatch—exerts the strongest influence on risk perception and, ultimately, on customer satisfaction and loyalty, will allow platform operators to prioritise their improvement efforts accordingly. For instance, if payment problems are found to be the primary driver of risk perception and post-purchase dissatisfaction, platform operators should invest in improving digital payment reliability, expanding payment options, streamlining refund processes, and reducing hidden charges. Conversely, if product quality mismatch is the dominant concern, the focus should shift to strengthening product listing standards, seller accountability, and quality assurance mechanisms.

The study's findings will also be relevant for businesses operating in the financial technology and digital payments space in Nepal. By documenting the nature and extent of payment-related problems in online shopping, the study provides fintech companies with a clear picture of the consumer pain points they need to address in order to build trust and expand digital payment adoption (Luo et al., 2025; Chapagain & Aryal, 2026).

### 1.6.3 Policy Significance

The study has important implications for policymakers and consumer protection bodies in Nepal. The e-commerce sector in Nepal currently operates in a relatively under-regulated environment, with limited consumer protection mechanisms compared to more developed markets (Chapagain & Aryal, 2026). The findings of this study will provide empirical evidence of the specific harms—financial losses from payment failures, victimisation through scams, and dissatisfaction from product mismatches—that Nepalese online consumers are experiencing, and will thus inform the development of targeted regulatory interventions. These might include mandatory escrow payment systems, seller verification requirements, product authenticity standards, strengthened fraud reporting mechanisms, and consumer education campaigns.

At a broader level, the study aligns with Nepal's national digital economy development agenda, contributing evidence that can support the design of policies aimed at building a safer, more trustworthy, and more consumer-friendly online marketplace. Strengthening consumer confidence in e-commerce is essential not only for the sustainable growth of the sector, but also for the achievement of broader financial inclusion and economic development goals. The Department of Commerce and Supplies, the Nepal Rastra Bank, the Nepal Telecommunications Authority, and the Nepal Cyber Bureau all have regulatory roles that intersect with the issues addressed by this study, and the findings are intended to be accessible and actionable for these institutions.

### 1.6.4 Social Significance

The study also carries social significance in that it gives voice to the experiences of ordinary Nepalese online shoppers who have encountered payment failures, fraud, and product disappointments without adequate recourse or redress. By systematically documenting the nature and prevalence of these experiences, the study contributes to a body of evidence that can support advocacy for stronger consumer rights in Nepal's digital marketplace. It also highlights the differential vulnerability of certain consumer groups—including less digitally literate consumers, first-time online buyers, and consumers in lower income brackets—who may be disproportionately affected by the problems under study and who have fewer resources to seek redress when things go wrong.

## 1.7 Scope of the Study

The study is geographically focused on online shoppers in the Kathmandu Valley, which comprises the three districts of Kathmandu, Lalitpur, and Bhaktapur. This geographic scope is justified by the fact that Kathmandu Valley is the primary hub of Nepal's e-commerce activity, hosting the largest concentration of internet users, online shopping platforms, and digital payment infrastructure (Devkota et al., 2021). It is also the area where the most e-commerce-related research in Nepal has been conducted, allowing for comparability with prior studies.

The study targets individuals who have made at least one online purchase within the past twelve months, using any platform including dedicated e-commerce websites (such as Daraz, Sasto Deal, Jeevee), social media platforms (such as Facebook and Instagram), or other digital channels. The sample includes consumers across gender, age, education, and occupation categories, with a focus on adults aged 18 and above.

The scope of the study is confined to the consumer side of the e-commerce transaction and does not examine the perspectives of sellers, platform operators, or regulators. The study examines five key constructs: payment issues, online scams, product quality mismatch, risk perception, and post-purchase behaviour (including satisfaction). The study is cross-sectional in nature, capturing consumer perceptions and experiences at a single point in time.

The study does not extend to rural or remote areas of Nepal, where e-commerce penetration remains low, nor does it address B2B (business-to-business) e-commerce transactions. The findings are therefore most directly applicable to the urban e-commerce consumer population of the Kathmandu Valley, though they may offer insights relevant to other urban centres in Nepal with similar characteristics, such as Pokhara, Biratnagar, and Lalitpur. The study is conducted within the framework of the Nepalese e-commerce context and does not attempt comparative analysis with other countries, though the theoretical framework employed draws on international literature and is designed to be applicable in other developing-economy settings.

## 1.8 Limitations of the Study

While the study is designed with rigour and care, several limitations must be acknowledged.

**Self-report bias:** Data are collected through a structured questionnaire relying on respondents' self-reported perceptions and experiences. Respondents may be subject to recall bias, social desirability bias, or may not accurately reflect actual behaviour, particularly regarding sensitive matters such as scam victimisation or dissatisfaction with specific sellers.

**Geographic limitation:** The study is restricted to the Kathmandu Valley and may not be representative of online shoppers in other urban or rural areas of Nepal. The e-commerce ecosystem, internet access, and consumer behaviour patterns in other parts of Nepal may differ significantly from those in Kathmandu.

**Cross-sectional design:** The cross-sectional nature of the study means that causal relationships between variables can be inferred but not definitively established. Longitudinal data would be needed to confirm causal direction and to track changes in consumer perceptions and behaviour over time.

**Platform heterogeneity:** The study treats the online shopping experience as a broadly uniform phenomenon, but in reality, consumer experiences may vary significantly across different platforms (e.g., Daraz vs. Facebook sellers vs. niche e-commerce sites). The aggregation of experiences across platforms may mask platform-specific patterns.

**Sampling limitations:** The use of a non-probability, convenience-based sampling approach limits the statistical generalisability of the findings to the broader population of Nepalese online shoppers. The sample is likely to over-represent educated, young, urban consumers with frequent online shopping experience.

**Rapidly changing context:** The e-commerce sector in Nepal is evolving rapidly, with new platforms, payment systems, and regulatory developments emerging continuously. The findings of this cross-sectional study reflect the situation at a particular moment in time and may not remain fully applicable as the sector matures.

These limitations are acknowledged to help readers calibrate the degree of confidence they place in the findings and to identify directions for future research that could address these gaps. Despite these limitations, the study represents a meaningful advance over prior Nepalese e-commerce research by integrating multiple problem dimensions within a single, theoretically grounded empirical model, and by focusing specifically on the post-purchase phase of the consumer experience—a phase that has received comparatively little attention in the Nepalese context despite its fundamental importance for customer retention and platform trust-building.

## 1.9 Definition of Terms

The following definitions are adopted for the key terms used throughout this study.

**Electronic commerce (e-commerce):** The buying and selling of goods and services through electronic platforms and web-based technologies, utilising information technology to enhance convenience, speed, and accessibility for consumers and businesses (Nguyen et al., 2025).

**Online shopping:** A subset of e-commerce in which individual consumers directly purchase goods and services from sellers via digital platforms, including dedicated e-commerce websites, mobile applications, and social media platforms, without physical contact between buyer and seller at the point of transaction.

**Payment issues:** Problems encountered by consumers during the payment stage of an online transaction, including payment failures or system errors, limited payment options, insecurity in providing financial information, hidden or unexpected charges, and slow or absent refund processes (Devkota et al., 2021; Pandey, 2025).

**Online scams:** Fraudulent or deceptive practices encountered in the context of online shopping, including non-delivery of purchased goods, sale of counterfeit or misdescribed products, phishing attacks, fraudulent payment gateways, and impersonation of legitimate sellers (Shrestha et al., 2025; Chapagain & Aryal, 2026).

**Product quality mismatch:** The discrepancy between the quality, specifications, or physical condition of a product as presented on an online platform and the product as actually received by the consumer, including cases of wrong items, damaged goods, poor quality, and misleading product descriptions (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021; Tanjung et al., 2025).

**Risk perception:** The consumer's subjective assessment of the likelihood and magnitude of an adverse outcome resulting from an online transaction, encompassing financial risk (potential monetary loss), product risk (failure of the product to meet expectations), privacy risk (risk of personal data exposure), and psychological risk (anxiety or discomfort associated with the transaction) (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008).

**Post-purchase behaviour:** The set of actions, evaluations, and responses exhibited by consumers following the completion of an online transaction, including complaining to the seller or platform, returning products, sharing negative experiences through word-of-mouth or online reviews, avoiding particular sellers or platforms, and modifying future purchase intentions (Tanjung et al., 2025; Izumi et al., 2025).

**Customer satisfaction:** The consumer's overall evaluation of the extent to which an online shopping experience meets or exceeds their pre-purchase expectations, reflecting the cumulative assessment of the transaction process, product quality, service quality, and post-purchase support (Hipólito et al., 2025; Pavlou, 2003).

**Consumer trust:** The consumer's subjective confidence in the reliability, integrity, and competence of an online seller or platform, which reduces the perceived uncertainty of online transactions and facilitates purchase intentions (Kim et al., 2008; Shrestha et al., 2025).

**Dark patterns:** User interface design choices in online shopping platforms that benefit the platform or seller by coercing, steering, or deceiving consumers into making unintended or harmful decisions, such as hidden charges, misleading subscription sign-ups, and false urgency claims (Mathur et al., 2019).

## 1.10 Organization of the Study

This study is structured into five chapters, each serving a distinct function in the overall research narrative.

**Chapter I: Introduction** provides the background and context for the study, articulating the growth and challenges of e-commerce in Nepal. It presents the statement of the problem, research objectives, research questions, hypotheses, significance, scope, and limitations of the study, and defines the key terms used throughout.

**Chapter II: Related Literature and Theoretical Framework** reviews the existing theoretical and empirical literature pertaining to the study's core constructs: payment issues, online scams, product quality mismatch, risk perception, post-purchase behaviour, and customer satisfaction. The chapter traces the intellectual lineage of each construct, reviews relevant studies in both international and Nepalese contexts, identifies gaps in the literature, and presents the conceptual framework that guides the empirical investigation.

**Chapter III: Research Methods** describes the methodological design of the study in detail. It explains the research design adopted, the population and sample, the sampling technique, the instrumentation used for data collection (including the survey questionnaire and its sources), the data collection procedure, and the statistical techniques employed for data analysis.

**Chapter IV: Analysis and Results** presents the findings of the empirical investigation. Descriptive statistics characterise the sample and the distribution of responses across the study's constructs. Inferential analyses—including correlation analysis, regression analysis, and mediation analysis—are used to test the research hypotheses. The results are presented systematically, with reference to the research questions and hypotheses developed in Chapter I.

**Chapter V: Discussion, Conclusion, and Implications** interprets the findings in light of the existing literature reviewed in Chapter II. It discusses the theoretical and practical implications of the results, acknowledges the study's limitations, and proposes directions for future research. Conclusions are drawn with respect to each research objective, and recommendations are offered for platform operators, policymakers, and consumer protection bodies in Nepal.

**Appendices** include the survey questionnaire administered to respondents, along with any additional supporting material.
