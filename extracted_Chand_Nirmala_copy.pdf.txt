Abstract 

This study aims to explore the impact of digital payment on consumer behavior in Ne-
pal. Following the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), the study investigates the 
impact of perceived ease of use, perceived usefulness, and security concerns on pay-
ment selection.  

The study collected data utilizing a quantitative research strategy and surveyed urban 
and rural consumers. Findings indicate wide utilization of digital payment, with mobile 
wallets being the most popular. Key drivers to usage include convenience, ease, and 
security. However, low internet connectivity and fear of fraud remain issues in rural ar-
eas.  

The results show huge differences in the rates of adoption of digital payments, with 
the most frequent users being youth, urban, and educated consumers. Most of them 
intended to use digital payments in the future, indicating growing familiarity despite 
existing challenges. The results are of specific importance to policymakers and finan-
cial institutions aiming to promote digital payments in Nepal’s emerging payment eco-
system.

Introduction 

1.1  Research Background 

The  rise  of  digital  payments  has  fundamentally  transformed  how  consumers  and  busi-

nesses engage with each other globally. Digital payments are defined as transactions com-

pleted for products or services that are either ordered online or offline and initiated via the 

Net. Gaining insights into how digital economic facilities influence buyer choices is becom-

ing increasingly important, particularly in developing countries such as Nepal. In Nepal, the 

Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) is committed to ensuring the safekeeping and efficiency of the 

country's National Payments System (NPS), acknowledging that payment functions are a 

vital part of a state's financial framework and are critical for its stability, the implementation 

of monetary policy, and the development of capital markets (Rijal 2024). 

As digital payment systems become more popular, they are changing the way consumers 

make transactions, which in turn affects their buying choices, preferences, and overall fi-

nancial habits. The rapid acceptance of digital payment options has been largely driven by 

developments  in  information  and  communication  technology  (ICT),  leading  to  the  rise  of 

various  methods  such  as  digital  wallets,  internet  banking,  and  mobile  banking  in  Nepal 

(Poudel et al. 2023; Magar et al. 2023a). This transition is illustrated by a significant rise in 

digital transactions, which increased to NPR 1,559 billion in 2019 from NPR 712 billion in 

2018. indicating an increasing acceptance and dependence on these systems by consum-

ers (Poudel et al. 2023). 

Demographic  factors  also  significantly  influence  consumer  perceptions  regarding  digital 

payment methods. Lohana and Roy (2023) noted that aspects such as age, gender, school-

ing, and revenue greatly impact the usage and fulfillment associated with digital payment 

systems. For example, younger users, who are generally more adept with technology, tend 

to have a greater inclination to embrace digital payment options compared to older individ-

uals, who may lean toward conventional cash transactions (Goetze et al. 2023). This gen-

erational gap highlights the necessity of comprehending consumer behavior to digital pay-

ment adoption, as it can guide efforts to improve user engagement and satisfaction (Magar 

et al. 2023). 

Furthermore, the COVID-19 pandemic also fast-tracked the transition to digital payments, 

as individuals aimed to reduce physical interactions and improve transaction safety (Daqar 

et al. 2021). The pandemic not only sped up the acceptance of digital payment systems but 

also shaped consumer attitudes and behaviors, resulting in a greater dependence on these 

technologies for routine transactions (Zhou 2022). As digital payments become increasingly

woven into daily routines, it is vital to comprehend their effects on consumer behavior in 

Nepal for both businesses and policymakers, as this knowledge can inform the creation of 

more effective financial services and consumer education programs (Bhattarai et al. 2023). 

1.2  Thesis Objectives, Research Question, and Limitations 

The research objective serves as a foundational element in the research process, guiding 

the direction and scope of the study. A well-defined study objective is crucial for ensuring

(Marken  and  Morrison  2013).  emphasize  the  importance  of  precise  definitions  and  con-

sistent terminology in educational research objectives, noting that this precision is essential 

for replicating studies and conducting comprehensive analyses over time.  

This thesis aims to determine changing consumer attitudes toward digital payments. The 

aim is to recognize the role of demographic factors such as gender, age, education, and 

income in shaping consumer views of digital payments. There is a lack of studies on how 

digital  payment  impacts  consumer  behavior  in  Nepal.  This  is  intended  to  deliver  deeper 

insights  into  the  acceptance  of  digital  payment  approaches  while  shopping,  based  on  a 

literature review and research. 

Formulating a clear and focused research question is essential, as it not only defines the 

scope of the investigation but also influences the design, data collection, and analysis pro-

cess (Aslam & Emmanuel 2010; Jain et al. 2023). 

The main research question of this thesis is: 

•  How do people use and perceive digital payments in Nepal?

1.3  Theoretical Framework 

The consumer decision-making process is a critical area of study, particularly in emerging 

markets like Nepal, where understanding the motivations behind consumer behavior can 

significantly  influence  marketing  strategies,  policymaking,  and  business  practices.  The 

Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) provides a comprehensive framework to explore the 

impact of digital payments on consumer behavior in Nepal. This analysis will delve into the 

economic, psychological, and technological factors that shape consumer decisions in the 

context of digital payment approval. 

Research shows that how consumers view the practicality of digital payment systems has 

a  great  influence  on  their  adoption  of  technology.  For  example,  it  has  been  proven  that 

individuals will approve mobile payment systems if they feel that the technologies will make 

transactions easier and faster (Su et al. 2018; Li et al. 2019). In addition to that, the usability 

of a digital payment system is important; when the system is easy to use for the customers, 

they will accept it (Leang et al. 2023; Li et al. 2019). Furthermore, Tang et al. (2021) high-

lighted that customers’ perception of how the system must work and their privacy in terms 

of ensuring the security of the information also come out as a major determinant of using 

payments.  

1.4  Research Methodology 

A research methodology describes the techniques and steps taken to collect and analyse 

information on a particular study topic. It is an organized, logical strategy that researchers 

employ to direct a study problem and achieve their objectives (Bahishti 2022). A methodol-

ogy outlines how an investigator will carry out their investigation to obtain precise and reli-

able data. It includes the types of data the researcher will gather, the sources of that data, 

and the methods for gathering and analysing it (Bahishti 2023). A research design behaves 

as  the  framework  for  the  study,  guiding  the  data  collection  and  analysis  processes,  the 

selection of research tools, and the sampling strategies to be employed (Tobi & Kampen 

4 

2018). This study adopts a deductive research approach, which starts with a theory or hy-

pothesis and seeks solutions through data collection and analysis. This approach is partic-

ularly suitable as the research focuses on quantitative data to examine the impact of digital 

payment systems on purchasing actions. The researcher will draw on existing theories and 

studies conducted on similar topics to identify the key factors influencing consumer behav-

iour in Nepal regarding the acceptance of digital payments. 

Digital payment serves as the focal subject of this research. The study incorporates both 

qualitative and quantitative sources of data. Qualitative data offers a deeper understanding 

of the underlying motivations and contextual factors, while quantitative data provides meas-

urable insights into patterns and trends (Arendt et al.2012). 

The research  utilizes  both  primary  and  secondary  data  collection methods.  Primary  data 

will be collected through a structured questionnaire distributed among individuals who ac-

tively use digital payment systems in Nepal. Secondary data will be gathered from newspa-

pers, articles, journals, and websites to gain insights from prior studies and reports on the 

adoption and impact of digital payments. The study aims to answer critical questions about 

how digital payment systems influence consumer purchasing behaviour in Nepal. The ques-

tionnaire includes a five-point Likert scale, ranking, multiple-choice, single-choice, and de-

mographic items. 

According  to  Magar  et  al  (2023b)  Nepal's  population  in  2024  is  projected  to  be  approxi-

mately 31.07 million, and the target population for this research comprises individuals who

collaborates  with  digital  payment  service  users  and  disseminates  the  survey.  Given  the 

challenge of reaching all users of digital payment systems, the researcher also leverages 

personal networks, including friends, family, and colleagues. The study seeks responses 

from at least 100 participants, including businesspersons, private and government employ-

ees,  students,  and  unemployed  individuals.  Respondents  must  be  at  least  18  years  old, 

reflecting the legal age for conducting financial transactions in Nepal. 

The initial focus of the research is to utilize surveys to store and analyse data on consumer 

behaviour. The validity and reliability of findings are ensured through careful questionnaire 

design and systematic data analysis. This method enables the study to remain concentrated 

on data clearly obtained from participants, providing a pure insight into how digital payment 

systems influence consumer behaviour in Nepal.

2  Technology acceptance model 

Davis’s Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (1989) is the most widely used model to ex-

plain how users adopt and use new technology. The most prevalent determinants that de-

cide an individual’s use decision for a technology, as proposed by this model, are Perceived 

Usefulness (PU) is the extent to which an individual believes that the use of a specific sys-

tem will improve his or her performance (Davis 1989). In mobile payments is the extent to 

which consumers believe that the use of electronic payment systems such as online bank-

ing or mobile wallets facilitates transactions in a more convenient, quicker, and easier way. 

Furthermore, Teker et al. (2022) note that convenience is the key driver of the growing use 

of digital payments by business customers as well as end consumers. In summary, TAM 

can explain why digital payment systems are being utilized in Nepal by their users. If they 

are  convenient  and  helpful  enough  to  the  users,  then  they  will  be  able  to  embrace  and 

continue to utilize these systems, influencing their overall behavior toward digital transac-

tions.

2.1  Perceived Usefulness 

According to Davis (1989)  Perceived Usefulness (PU) is the extent to which an individual 

thinks that utilizing a specific system will increase his/her work performance. In the context 

of digital payments, perceived usefulness means how much consumers believe that using 

digital payment systems (e.g., mobile wallets, online banks, etc.) is a better way of dealing 

with money, saving time, and making them more comfortable shopping. Therefore, the key 

success indicator of digital payment systems is perceived utility. Many studies illustrate how 

security, convenience, and speed influence digital payment users’ satisfaction and under-

standing of digital payments. When digital payment functionalities meet users’ expectations, 

(Beura  et  al.  2023).  the  claim  that  satisfaction  is  lifted  and  usage  continues  (Sholihah  &

Ariyani 2023.) In this regard, (Aditya & Ekyawan 2021.) also, establish a solid positive con-

nection between user gratification and digital payment adoption, namely, users engaging in 

digital payment will more likely stay engaged. (Teker et al. 2022). Also, back up this point 

by arguing that ease is one of the key reasons digital payments are becoming increasingly 

popular among consumers and businesses. 

Another consideration regards security, which has a primordial role in forming perceptions 

of usefulness. Both as it relates to digital security awareness and because it highlights the 

importance of trust in security measures, Jusman and Fauziah (2024) claim that it is the 

lack of awareness of digital security that deters users from fully using cashless payment 

methods. Muhtasim et al. (2022) also point out that perceived security strongly determines 

customer satisfaction, which leads to the additional fact that perceived safety substantially 

affects digital payment adoption. The study by (Yaokumah, Kumah, & Okai 2017). shows 

how  demographic  factors  shape  the  usage  of  e-payment  services  using  the  lens  of  the 

Technology Acceptance Model (TAM). Perceived usefulness can also be improved by im-

proving educational initiatives, as Behera et al. (2023) note that higher levels of financial 

education help increase awareness and adoption rates due to a lowering of technological 

apprehension. 

However, the acceptance of digital payment techniques in Nepal depends on demographic, 

economic, and technological factors. From NPR 712 billion in 2018 to NPR 1,559 billion in 

2019, digital payments had spread throughout the country with the use of digital payment 

platforms like ConnetIPS, Mowobil wallets, QR codes, etc. (Poudel et al. 2023). As Lohana 

& Roy (2021) state, the satisfaction of the consumer and the adoption rate of the product 

are  variables  —  i.e.,  it  depends  on  the  consumer’s  age,  gender,  education,  and  income 

level. Moreover, the impact of the integration of digital payment systems in governance has 

enhanced their perceived usefulness by promoting transparency, efficiency, and especially 

in remote areas, as Kc and Kc (2024) argue. Likewise, supervision by the regulatory au-

thorities of Nepal Rastra Bank is necessary for security and consumer confidence (Rijal and 

Thapa, 2024). Nevertheless, security risks and lack of awareness persist, which is why tar-

geted educational initiatives are still extremely important to grow trust and increase adoption 

(Meher et al. 2021). 

In addition, digital payments cannot be overlooked in how they impact consumer behaviour. 

Recent studies have found that the convenience and efficiency that digital payment system 

provides to consumers are very important and affect the consumers' choice and purchase 

decisions  (Bhattarai  et  al.  2023;  Rijal  &  Thapa,  2024).  So,  the  consumption  of  Nepalese 

products has been in sync with the rise of e-commerce and mobile commerce, together with 

the  surge  in digital  payments,  since customers want  to  have the  essential  experience  of 

seamless transactions (Rijal & Thapa, 2024). These perceived risks, such as the security 

risk or the fact that consumers are not familiar with it, slow the approval of digital payments

2.2  Perceived ease of use  

The perception users have about how easy it will be to use digital payment systems influ-

ences their adoption of these systems in developing nations, including Nepal. Different sys-

tem components and accessibility levels determine users' judgment of how effortless their 

interaction with the system will be. Research indicates that ease of use perception stands 

as the foundation for how users accept technology based on their attitudes toward adoption 

(Özkan  et  al.  2010).  The  Simplicity,  along  with  ease  of  use  on  digital  payment  systems 

including PayPal, helps platforms achieve success by using intuitive designs according to 

Teoh et al. (2013). Nepalese citizens who are less familiar with technology require inter-

faces that are easy to use for adopting new platforms. 

Research from Nepal provides complete evidence of how users decide about digital pay-

ments based on security measures and privacy protection. The security-related concerns 

perceived  by  young  Pokhara Metropolitan  City  residents  surpassed  the advantages  they 

felt digital payments would provide, according to Poudel et al. (2023). Users require better 

security safeguards as they also value easy interfaces for payment platforms since these 

mechanisms provide peace of mind about the safety of their funds. The evaluation of plat-

form ease during use acts as an essential condition for perceived usefulness since users 

find  effective  platforms through  convenient  interfaces,  according to Lai  &  Ahmad (2015). 

The development of digital payment adoption among Nepalese users demands a user-ori-

ented interface since they must transition from cash to electronic transaction systems. Dig-

ital payment education, along with awareness programs, allows users to view digital pay-

ment  systems  as  user-friendly.  Knowledge  lessons  about  digital  system  features  enable 

users to grasp the technology better for increased acceptance rates (Sivathanu, 2019). Dig-

ital payment systems have become simpler to operate using modern financial sector tech-

nology to prevent risks associated with traditional cash payments, as reported by Budiarti 

et al. (2021). Digital payments in Nepal will thrive through developer improvements in plat-

form user experience, coupled with sustained customer education about systems that cre-

ate trustworthy user relationships (Khan et al. 2023; Sholihah & Ariyani, 2023).

Intention of use 

The Digital payment system usage research has gained importance because modern tech-

nology  norms  are  changing  along  with  consumer  conduct.  The  concept  depends  on  the 

Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), which shows that users' technology adoption inten-

tions  depend  strongly  on  how  much  they  perceive  the  system  aids  them  (PU)  and  how 

straightforward its operation is (PEOU). User intention toward digital payment systems de-

pends mainly on performance expectancy, combined with social influence together with fa-

cilitating conditions, and security and privacy concerns, as shown by continued research. 

The factors that form user perceptions serve as essential drivers for mobile wallet adoption 

alongside e-payment system implementation and other digital payment alternatives (Linge 

et al. 2023; Patil et al. 2020; Gupta & Arora, 2019). 

Users choose to adopt digital payment solutions because they view technological tools as 

useful according to performance expectancy definitions. User adoption of digital payment 

solutions increases based on their perception of these solutions being beneficial and effi-

cient,  according  to  Linge  et  al.  (2023)  and  Patil  et  al.  (2020).  Users'  adoption  decisions 

regarding technology are greatly influenced by the impact important others have on such 

decisions through social influence. Beura et al. (2023), alongside Simatupang et al. (2022) 

stress  that  peer  conduct  and  social  guidelines  drive  adoption  choices  according  to  their 

research, while Tian et al. (2023) examine the mutual effects of trust strength with service 

quality and social influence for digital payment system use. 

Necessary resources, together with modern infrastructure, serve as important enabling fac-

tors  when  implementing  successful  digital  payment  systems.  According  to  Poudel  et  al. 

(2023) and Nurdin et al. (2023) reliable technology access with strong internet connectivity 

enables users to make effective use of digital payment systems. Consumers refrain from 

adopting  digital  payments  because  of  their  strong  concerns  about  security  and  privacy. 

Adoption of digital payments heavily depends on perceived trustworthiness, together with 

security measures, according to Nguyen et al. (2016), along with Flavián et al. (2020) and 

Ariffin et al. (2021) in their research. The digital payment acceptance process in Nepal pro-

vides isolated views into consumer conduct due to socioeconomic factors combined with 

technological elements and cultural components. Digital payment systems continue to ex-

pand  rapidly  because  mobile  technology  penetration  extends  beyond  traditional  banking 

systems  toward  digital  methods  of  payment  (Poudel  et  al.  2023).  The  adoption  of  digital 

payment platforms for daily financial transactions occurs because these systems provide 

convenience, security, and seamless integration into daily activities (Shrestha, 2024). The 

COVID-19 pandemic escalated adoption through digital infrastructure requirements despite

issues  with  digital  literacy  and  limited  infrastructure  (Kurian  2021).  The  government,  to-

gether with regulatory institutions and the Nepal Rastra Bank, must play essential roles to 

guarantee a secure digital payment system that will boost consumer trust and promote na-

tionwide economic development (Rijal & Thapa, 2024; Bhattarai et al.2023). 

2.4  Actual use 

Digital payment utilization (AU) describes consumer interaction with online banking and mo-

bile wallet systems where transactions take place. Users generally adopt digital payments 

because these systems offer both easy usability and enhanced convenience. The Indian 

adoption of these systems during demonetization times mainly depended on user conven-

ience because that element matched the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Tech-

nology (UTAUT) as Sivathanu (2019) explains. The research by Poudel et al. (2023) states 

that students in Nepal need technology access coupled with sufficient knowledge to suc-

cessfully adapt new practices. Ummasyroh et al. (2024) establish that consumers choose 

to maintain digital payment practices when these systems prove easy to operate and ben-

eficial for their needs. 

The evaluation of subjective risks between customers plays an extremely important role in 

forming AU. Chaveesuk et al. (2021) showed that perceived risk functions as a main obsta-

cle towards digital payment acceptance in Thailand, whereas Nasiketha et al. (2023) pro-

vided similar evidence for Sri Lanka, as perceived risk levels directly influence digital pay-

ment  adoption.  Consumers  who  understand  the  benefits  of  digital  payments  will  choose 

adoption  based  on  how  well  they  manage  associated  risks,  which  is  influenced  by  their 

access to facilitating conditions like technology. AU's adoption is influenced by social influ-

ence along with financial literacy levels, although these come from external sources. Social 

status influences purchasing behaviour according to Rifa'Ie and Chan (2024), and Oktafian 

(2022) makes clear that financial education determines effective digital payment usage. 

Different regions of the world have distinguished contexts for digital payments, which makes 

AU more difficult to achieve. The growth of digital transactions in India was propelled by 

technological  advancements  and  cashless  commerce  developments,  according  to  Khan 

(2021), although Ligon et al. (2019) explain that Jaipur shows low digital payment adoption 

because of poor infrastructure and low awareness levels. Population statistics such as age 

level, educational attainment, and income affect digital payment behaviour in Nepal based 

on Lohana and Roy (2021). Customer satisfaction rates explored by Aditya and Ekyawan 

(2021), along with the advanced services of Khalti and IME Pay secure remote transactions

(Shrestha 2024.) drive users to continue their usage according to research. The digital di-

vide remains active throughout Nepal because it creates different internet accessibility lev-

tecting digital transaction security (KC & KC, 2024; Rijal & Thapa, 2024.)

3.1  Empirical Research 

 This  research  examines  the  effect  of  digital  payments  on  Nepalese  consumer  behavior 

using a quantitative approach. It is based on the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Da-

vis 1989.) where the concepts of perceived usefulness, ease of use, intention to use, and 

actual use exist. The study tests these variables to find out the attitude of consumers toward 

digital payment systems. The investigation finds out how people perceive the efficacy and 

security of digital payments, and how demographic factors such as age, gender, education, 

and income affect the payment systems. The study's main question is: “How do people use 

and perceive digital payments in Nepal?” Data was gathered using Microsoft Forms through 

a closed-ended questionnaire to better understand the trends and issues related to digital 

payment  adoption.  The  findings  can  help businesses and  policymakers by offering infor-

mation about how digital payments are being accepted and used in Nepal. 

3.2  Data Collection 

The information was collected by using Microsoft Forms through a closed-ended question-

naire on people in Nepal who use or can use digital payment systems like mobile wallets 

(eSewa, Khalti, Fonepay), internet or mobile banking, and debit/credit cards. The  partici-

pants  were  a  mix  of  businesspersons,  employed  individuals,  students,  and  unemployed 

persons aged 18 years and above, the legal age for financial transactions in Nepal. Multiple-

choice, single-choice, and demographic questions were employed in the survey to identify 

users’ habits, preferences, and perceptions of effectiveness, security, and problems. 

To attain a high response rate of participants, the survey was shared through personal net-

works (family, friends, and colleagues) and social media. The survey took place from April 

28 to May 1, 2025, and received 151 responses, out of which 139 complete responses were 

used for the study. The respondents were from urban (91 from cities) and rural (60 from 

towns/villages) areas. There were 98 men and 53 women in the group. Most of the respond-

ents  were  aged  25-34  years  (78)  followed  by  18-24  (44),  35-50  (23),  and  under  18  (6). 

Education  levels  ranged  from  school  (71)  to  graduate  (46)  and  postgraduate  and  above 

(34). Income levels ranged widely, and 37 did not wish to reveal their income. Anonymity 

was maintained to obtain honest responses. The final sample size surpassed the target of 

100, which contributed to additional strength to the study findings.

3.3  Data Analysis 

 The results of the survey were analysed with basic statistical analysis to see how people 

in Nepal are using digital payments, how they feel about them, and what might be discour-

aging others from using them. The focus was the frequency of usage of digital payments, 

where they are utilized, why they are utilized, how convenient and secure they are perceived 

to be, and how demographic characteristics like age or income affect usage. The findings 

below are based on 139 complete questionnaires.

3.3.5  Key Insights  

The  information  points  out  that  payments  through  electronic  media  are  slowly  but  surely 

becoming the order of the day in Nepal. They are quick, secure, and easy, especially if they 

are being paid via mobile wallets, since they are readily available and very popular to boot. 

Not everyone does that as much, though. Youths, males, urban residents, and those who 

are better educated and richer are likely to make use of electronic payment. Problems such 

as  unreliable  internet  connectivity  and  the  fear  of  online  scams  keep  others,  particularly 

rural residents, away from using digital methods. These results confirm the Technology Ac-

ceptance Model (TAM), that is, that the ease and convenience that a technology is seen as 

are the factors that can be employed to predict the likelihood that individuals will adopt it. 

Trust and user history are also important in adoption. 

 
 
 
21 

High-Level Adoption of Digital Payments: Most of the respondents (around 92%) indi-

cated the usage of digital payments, and around 66% of them utilize it daily. It suggests that 

the digital payment means are well-adopted in Nepal. Among the options available, mobile 

wallets like eSewa, Khalti, and Fonepay were most widely utilized (56.3%), followed by in-

ternet  or  mobile  banking  (32.6%),  and  debit/credit  cards  (11.1%).  This  kind  of  popularity 

must  be  credited  to  mobile  wallets  being  more  convenient  and  easily  accessible  to  use 

(Shrestha, 2024). This finding confirms to previous evidence of the rise of online transac-

tions in Nepal, from NPR 712 billion in 2018 to NPR 1,229 billion in 2019 (Poudel et al., 

2023). 

Role of Speed and Ease of Use: Individuals like making payments online most because 

they  are  convenient  (46%),  easy  (24.5%),  and  safe  (21.6%).  These  are  the  factors  that 

justify the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) that individuals will use technology if they 

experience it as useful (Beura et al., 2023). Most of the respondents also said that electronic 

payments  are  easy  to  use,  where  (54.8%)  said  they  were  “very  easy”  and  (37.8%)  said 

“easy”.  This  shows  how  ease  of  use  is  important  in  encouraging  the  use  of  these  tools, 

especially applications like Khalti, which is designed to be easy to use (Teoh et al., 2013). 

Demographic Differences in Usage: Demographic factors significantly affected the adop-

tion of digital payments. Younger age groups (18-24 and 25-34) indicated higher daily usage 

(over 60%), as well as males (74.2% versus 25.8% of females), urban residents (58.9%), 

and those with higher incomes (e.g., 8.6% for over Rs. 100,000). However, rural residents 

(41.1%) and less educated (47% for school level) indicated lower adoption. 

Barriers to More Extensive Use: The Most substantial barriers to more regular usage of 

digital payments included lack of internet connectivity (50%), fear of fraud/hacking (46.8%), 

and preference for cash (21%). All these barriers were larger in rural areas, indicating trust 

problems hindering adoption (Meher et al. 2021). 

Strong Belief in Security: Approximately 77 respondents (55.4%) strongly agreed that e-

payment systems are secure, which shows trust in regulatory interventions by the Nepal 

Rastra Bank (Rijal & Thapa, 2024). However, 19 respondents (13.7%) were neutral, sug-

gesting  a  need  for  more  trust-building  measures,  particularly  for  less-educated  and  rural 

consumers (Jusman & Fauziah 2024.) 

Retailer Acceptance and Accessibility: About 68.7% of the participants confirmed that 

digital payments are widely acceptable by retailers, particularly in urban areas, and hence 

they can easily be used in day-to-day transactions. However, 24.6% were uncertain, while 

6.7% disagreed, pointing to gaps in rural merchant adoption that compromise accessibility 

(Aryal, 2021).

4  Conclusion 

 The study analyzed the effect of digital payment via online mediums on consumer behavior 

in  Nepal,  i.e.,  on  mobile  wallet  (eSewa,  Khalti,  Fonepay),  internet/mobile  banking,  and 

debit/credit cards. Following the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) of (Davis 1989), the 

study analysed how perceived usefulness (PU), perceived ease of use (PEOU), intention 

to use, and actual use affect adoption. The survey was organized and collected feedback 

from 151 respondents from rural and urban areas. The survey revealed a high usage rate, 

with most customers considering digital payments safe and convenient. But there is still a 

problem regarding poor internet connectivity and  fear of cyber fraud (Poudel et al.2023). 

Demographic characteristics like age, educational level, gender, and income also affected 

the usage behavior among the youth, educated, and city residents, with increased adoption 

observed  among  them  (Bhattarai  et  al.2023).  Results  present  pragmatic  implications  to 

companies, policymakers, and providers of digital services for enhancing digital payment 

systems and access to finance in Nepal. 

4.1  Answers to Research Questions 

This thesis aims to examine how Digital payments are affecting consumer behavior in Ne-

pal. It focuses on understanding how people use digital payment methods and what they 

think about them. The main question of research is: “How do people use and perceive digital 

payments in Nepal?” To help answer this, the study also explores two sub-questions: “How 

do consumer perceptions of efficiency influence their attitudes towards digital payments?” 

And “How do demographic factors like age, gender, education, and income shape adoption 

and usage  patterns  of  digital  payments?”  The  goal  is  to  understand the current  situation 

and how different people in Nepal are adapting to digital payments.  

The first sub-questions were: How do consumers’ perceptions of efficiency influence their 

attitudes towards digital payments?  

Most users view digital payments as convenient, quick, and safe. These positive attitudes 

lead them to choose digital payments over the traditional payment mode (cash). The Tech-

nology Acceptance Model assumes that people will adopt technology if it is seen to save 

time and is not difficult to use. This can be found from the feedback because most of the 

respondents  thought  that  digital  payment  systems  are  easy  to  use.  Khalti  services  were 

much appreciated for ease of use. Most of the users also felt secure about digital payments, 

perhaps because of government regulation. But issues surrounding the security of these 

systems continued to exist, and so there is more education and awareness needed, espe-

cially among rural residents or lower literacy. 

24 

The second sub-question was: How do demographic factors like age, gender, education, 

and income shape adoption and usage patterns of digital payments? 

Young adults were found to be more than likely to have greater use of digital payment in-

struments. Men proved to be more frequent users than women, and this might have been 

due to levels of access to technology or computer skills. Urban residents were also making 

higher payments online than rural residents, perhaps due to higher availability of internet 

and infrastructure. Levels of education and income also impact digital payment adoption—

higher educated, higher income individuals were more actively using digital payments daily, 

and less educated or less income individuals were less likely to do so. All these trends show 

that young, urban, educated, and better-off segments were increasingly embracing digital 

payments 

The  main  research  question  of  the  thesis  was:  How  do  people  use  and  perceive  digital 

payments in Nepal?  

Overall, internet payments have become a widespread and accepted way of life. People 

use digital wallets like eSewa, Khalti, and Fonepay as a daily habit, followed by mobile and 

internet banking. Convenience in using apps and the prevalence of smartphones have en-

couraged this trend. But there are some issues. Such as low internet penetration in some 

areas, fraud concerns, and cash requirements, especially in rural areas. Although electronic 

payment facilities are among the options for acceptance by most merchants, there aren't so 

many  individuals  who  are  aware  of  where  to  seek  such  facilities,  and  hence,  coverage 

among merchants needs to be widened. To their credit, however, most of them believe in 

the viability of electronic payments. They all aim to make more use of them in the short term 

and anticipate these systems finally substituting or even doing away with cash usage. That 

has  enormous  growth  potential  as  awareness,  confidence,  and  facilities  increase 

across the country. 

4.2  Validity and Reliability  

It is crucial to know validity and reliability because they help researchers ensure the findings 

are  accurate, reliable,  and  concerning the  rights  of the  participants.  These are  crucial  to 

ensuring the overall quality and integrity of a study. Validity is the research that measures 

what  it  is  supposed  to  and  that  the  findings  are  correct  and  credible.  This  study  used  a 

structured questionnaire based on the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), focusing on 

four  main  areas:  perceived  usefulness (PU),  perceived  ease  of  use (PEOU),  intention to 

use, and actual use (Davis, 1989). There was a mix of question types in the survey: a five-

point Likert scale (e.g., from “Strongly Disagree” to “Strongly Agree” for trust in security, and 

25 

“Very Difficult” to “Very Easy” for ease of use), multiple-choice, single-choice, and demo-

graphic questions. This was required to capture information about people's behavior and 

attitudes towards digital payments (Tobi & Kampen, 2018). 

To ensure content validity, refine questions to avoid uncertainty, and the scale’s ability to 

measure intended variables like perceived ease (54.8% said “very easy”) and security trust 

(55.4% agreed or strongly agreed) was enhanced. Moreover, external validity was assisted 

by the heterogeneous sample of 151, including urban (91) and rural (60) residents, male 

(98) and female (53), and mixed age, education, and income levels, though generalizability 

is limited only in Nepal’s unique context. Reliability was maintained through a systematic 

data collection process on Microsoft Forms from April 28 to May 1, 2025, with anonymity to 

minimize response bias. The sample size (139) was over the target of 100, improving sta-

tistical reliability (Tobi & Kampen 2018.) 

4.3  Suggestion for future research  

 Future work could use a qualitative or mixed-method approach, with a survey supported by 

interviews to provide a fuller reason why some are frightened of fraud or still wish to use 

cash.  This  would  provide more  valuable  information  regarding  consumer  attitudes  to  the 

convenience and safety of digital payments. Larger and more rural representative samples 

from research could bridge the gap in knowledge between rural and urban users. A com-

parison  of  Nepal  with  other  developing  countries  can  perhaps  illustrate  how  cultural  and 

economic variations impact the use of digital payments.

5  Summary 

The study examines the impact of digital payment on consumer behavior in Nepal, such as 

adoption trends, users’ perception, and key concerns. Based on the TAM, the study inves-

tigates  how  perceived  usefulness,  ease  of  use,  intended  behavior,  and  actual  use  drive 

digital payment adoption. A survey of 151(139 valid responses) consumers sowed that 92% 

use  online  payments,  of  which  mobile  wallets  (eSews,  Khalti,  Fonepay)  (56.3%),  inter-

net/mobile banking (32.6%), and credit/debit cards (11.1%) are the most widely used. The 

research concludes that young (18-34), urban, and educated individuals are the most fre-

quent consumers, while rural individuals and less technologically oriented people of Nepal 

are facing challenges such as the absence of proper internet connectivity as well as safety-

related issues. Convenience (46%), easy (24.5%), and security (21.6%) were the leading 

reasons for acceptance, and 92.6% of them support that digital payments are easy to use. 

Although 55.4% strongly believe that digital payments are secure, 46.8% remain concerned 

about fraud or hacking, particularly in rural areas. Digital payments will be more widely used 

in the future, as 71.1% of the participants mentioned, and 50% believe digital payments can 

replace cash one day, and 44% are not sure.  

The study confirms that perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness are the key factors 

that  facilitate  adoption,  according  to  TAM  principles.  However,  infrastructural  issues  and 

trust  gaps  must  be  addressed  to  enhance  digital  payment  adoption  by  enhancing  digital 

literacy, cybersecurity, and better connectivity in rural areas. Future research could utilize 

qualitative information for more deeper understanding or cross-country comparisons to en-

sure  cultural  and  economic  effects.  Overall,  the  digital  payment  in  Nepal  has  enormous 

growth potential if accessibility and security concerns are addressed appropriately.
