## Section 1: Nature of the GRP

### Introduction

These Guidelines are prepared and designed to help MBA students of SOMTU in the preparation of their GRP report. The guidelines address only matters of format and presentation such as arrangement of content, paper, spacing, headings, data analysis, interpretation and referencing. It is the responsibility of each student to ensure that his or her work conforms to the Guidelines set out below. The final GRP is approved by the concerned supervisor and the Research Committee with regards to questions of quality and content. Further clarification of the Guidelines can be obtained from the Director of SOMTU.

### Objectives of the GRP

The graduate research project (GRP) is an integral part of the postgraduate studies at SOMTU. Towards the end of your study at SOMTU, you are required to undertake a research assignment and prepare an integrative research report in any areas of management as approved by SOMTU.

The GRP explores business questions as they relate to industries, business climate, and management practices, focusing on findings in related research plus the methodological alternatives. The GRP thus involves conceptualizing, planning, implementing and writing up a report, which extends knowledge in the subject area under investigation.

This assignment specifically aims to develop knowledge, skills and attitudes necessary for conduct of individual research at a level which will make a distinct contribution to knowledge. You are expected to demonstrate the use of appropriate research methodology, and written skills through the preparation and presentation of a substantial investigation.

Specifically, the objectives of the GRP include:

- To provide an opportunity for the student to integrate classroom knowledge and practice.
- To enable graduate students to do an independent study to reflect a creative endeavour that can make a significant contribution to knowledge in a given field.
- To develop student's ability to read professional literature, report and other works critically in their design, treatment of data, and conclusion.
- To strengthen the ability of students in presenting their research work in a clear, concise and logical manner and enable other readers to use the results of their investigations.

### Activities Involved in the GRP

The following activities will be involved in the GRP:

- Selecting a relevant topic or issue for study.
- Locating the relevant literature.
- Locating the sources of information.
- Extracting the relevant information from these sources.
- Identifying the various dimensions of the problem or issue.
- Organizing and analyzing the data effectively.
- Drawing inferences and conclusions.
- Writing a report.

### Study Requirements

Your GRP report shall comply with the following requirements:

- The proposed field of study or topic of research must be approved by the concerned supervisor and the Director of SOMTU.
- The work must comply with any requirements as advised by the concerned supervisor.
- The work in a GRP must reach a satisfactory standard of expression and presentation.
- You must maintain close and regular contact with your supervisor and the Director of SOMTU.

### Reporting Requirements

You shall prepare a GRP report embodying the results of your research. The report submitted by you shall:

- Be an accurate account of research.
- Relate to the approved research topic.
- Not include work which has been submitted for any other academic award.
- Be written in English.
- Achieve a satisfactory standard of expression and presentation.
- Acknowledge any substantial assistance provided to you during the conduct of the research and writing the report.
- Conform to the rules and format of SOMTU for the presentation of the GRP report.

### Length of the GRP Report

The length for the GRP report shall be around **18,000–20,000 words** (approximately 70–75 pages). This length is exclusive of the materials included in appendices.

### Multiple Copies Required

- You shall submit **two loose-bound copies** of the GRP report to SOMTU for evaluation. After satisfactorily completing all recommended corrections, you shall submit **two hard-bound copies** and **one electronic copy**. Each hard copy shall be bound black.
- The duplicate copies of the original are to be produced using a method which gives a clear and permanent copy (laser copy). The use of spirit duplication or carbon copy is not acceptable.

### Evaluation of the GRP Report

The GRP report shall be evaluated by two examiners, one of whom shall be a faculty member of SOMTU. The internal examiners shall be appointed by the Director of SOMTU and the external examiner by the Examination section, Office of the Dean, Faculty of Management in consultation with the Director of SOMTU.

You will be required to attend the **viva-voce examination** and defend your work. You will also be required to give a seminar presentation of your report as organized by SOMTU. The weightage given for viva and the GRP report will be **40%** and **60%** respectively.

## Section 2: GRP Proposal

- Having to prepare the proposal for your GRP is part of your graduate training. It is an opportunity to organize your thoughts about your research topic, to decide how you will pursue the work, and to spell out what resources (financial, material, and technical) you need to carry out the research. A GRP proposal seeks to convince the supervisor(s) or the Graduate Research Committee (GRC) that the research project is feasible.
- The writing of a project proposal is an exercise that you will repeat many times in your professional career. The ability to "sell" a project convincingly is a crucial part of your toolbox of skills.
- A clearly defined research problem (or question) is central to the success of a research project. It helps you to determine that your project is doable before you begin writing the GRP report.
- The proposal should explicitly state the problem being addressed or gap in knowledge to be filled, describe the objectives and research techniques to be employed, and include a review of the principal relevant published literature.
- The proposal needs a thread of logic. It should build from a statement of the research problem or gap in knowledge, and follow an outline of detailed objectives that must be achieved (or questions that must be answered) if the problem is to be solved. The presentation of methodology should be clearly connected to the stated objectives.

A GRP proposal usually contains some formulations of the following sections:

1. Statement of the research problem
2. Research objectives
3. Research questions or hypotheses
4. Rationale for the research project
5. Related literature
6. Theoretical/conceptual framework
7. Methodological design – a plan outlining how and when each step of the GRP will be done
8. Expected outcomes
9. References

- Maximum length of the proposal should not exceed **3,000 words** (excluding references, figures or tables).
- GRP proposals shall be reviewed by the GRC of SOMTU. The GRC may either (a) accept the proposal as written or (b) return the proposal for revision.
- Although you are expected to seek guidance from your supervisor in the choice of topic and the method of solving the problem involved, the responsibility for the proposal writing lies with you. You will, as far as possible, work independently and demonstrate the ability to plan and outline an acceptable research project.
- Upon approval of the proposal by the Research Committee, you will be enrolled in the GRC.

## Section 3: GRP Reporting Format

As an MBA student at SOMTU, you are required to write reports for different project assignments in different courses. However, the GRP report writing is different from other reports. It is an organized, issue-focused, evidence-based and creative piece of academic writing. Hence, the following structure and guidelines have to be followed while preparing your GRP report.

### Structure of the GRP

A GRP report is typically made up of three main divisions: (1) Preliminary, (2) Body, and (3) Supplementary. Each of the sections contains different kinds of contents.

#### Preliminary Materials

- Title page of the GRP
- Certification
- Declaration of Authenticity
- Acknowledgments
- Table of Contents
- List of Tables and Figures
- Common Abbreviations Used
- Executive Summary

#### Body of the Report

- **Chapter I:** Introduction
- **Chapter II:** Related Literature and Theoretical Framework
- **Chapter III:** Research Methods
- **Chapter IV:** Analysis and Results
- **Chapter V:** Discussion, Conclusion and Implications

#### Supplementary Materials

- References or Bibliography
- Appendices

### Body of the GRP Report

- **Introduction** – Background information on the topic so that you are able to 'place' your research in the context. Details are given of your problem statement, objectives, hypotheses, scope and significance, definition of terms, limitations and an outline of the structure of the GRP report.

- **Related Literature and Theoretical Framework** – You must carefully structure your findings of the literature survey. It may be useful to do a chronological format where you discuss from the earliest to the latest research, placing your research appropriately in the chronology. Alternately, you could write in a thematic way, outlining the various themes that you discovered in the research regarding the topic. At the end of this chapter, you present your theoretical framework, briefly explaining the measurement of variables.

- **Research Method** – You have to clearly outline what methodology you used in your research i.e. what you did and how you did it. It must be clearly written so that it would be easy for another researcher to duplicate your research if they wished to. The contents of this chapter may include research design, population and sample, instrumentation sources and methods of data collection and data analysis.
  - It is usually written in a *passive* voice (e.g. "the participants were asked to fill in the questionnaire attached in Appendix 1") rather than an *active* voice (e.g. "I asked the participants to fill in the questionnaire").
  - Clearly reference any material you have used from other sources. Clearly label and number any diagrams, charts, and graphs. You do not include or discuss the results here.

- **Analysis and Results** – Data are analyzed statistically and results are presented and interpreted. Hypotheses are tested. This is where you indicate what you found in your research and give your interpretation. It will relate back to your literature review and your introductory GRP problem statement.

- **Discussion, Conclusion and Implications**
  - *Discussion of the Findings* – This is the most important section of your GRP report. This is your opportunity to show that you have understood the significance of your findings and that you are capable of applying theory in an independent manner. The discussion consists of argumentation: you investigate a phenomenon from several different perspectives and consider different interpretations.
  - *Conclusions and Implications* – This includes key facts from your research findings to help explain your results. You have to summarize, compare and evaluate your research results in the context of existing theories, and make comments about its success and effectiveness. You need to inform the reader of any unexpected findings or patterns that emerged from the data, and report a range of evidence to support assertions or interpretations presented. You could also indicate areas where your research has limits and where further research would be useful.

## Section 4: Reporting the Data and Presenting Results

Once you have obtained, entered and tabulated the data collected from the field, the next step is to analyze and interpret the results.

### Presenting the Data Tables

- Tables are one of the most efficient ways of presenting data. A table can hold far more information than a chart and remain intelligible.
- Tables should be used wherever:
  - There is a considerable amount of information which would become over-complicated in a chart or diagram.
  - The figures in the table are important and the reader may require them for further computations.
  - A table should quickly communicate a pattern of information to the reader.

**Example: Improved Table Construction**

*Figure 4.2 – Reasons for Dissatisfaction with Insurance Policies*

| Cause of dissatisfaction with policy | Total % | Motor % | Accident/Sickness % | Holiday % |
|--------------------------------------|---------|---------|----------------------|-----------|
| Cost of policy/premium increase | 33 | 39 | 27 | 11 |
| Delay in dealing with claim | 21 | 20 | 19 | 29 |
| Claim not met in full | 10 | 7 | 17 | 17 |
| Special conditions imposed | 7 | 6 | 7 | 7 |
| Claim not covered by policy | 5 | 2 | 10 | 15 |
| Misled into unnecessary/expensive policy | 3 | 1 | 16 | 0 |
| Other complaints | 22 | 27 | 13 | 10 |
| Not stated | 10 | 6 | 11 | 24 |
| **Sample size (dissatisfied)** | **210** | **149** | **28** | **33** |

*\*Multi-response, therefore total exceeds 100%*
*Source: United Management Consultants (2004). Attitudes of policyholders towards insurance policies, Kathmandu.*

### Reporting the Data

- Be as specific as possible about who is reporting and what they are reporting on. Use wording as similar to the question asked as possible.
- Report results from largest to smallest for multiple questions related to one another.
- Know when to report in percentages and when to report in numbers. In general, use percentages for samples with more than 10 respondents; use numbers for samples with fewer than 10.
- Keep tables and graphs as clear and simple as possible.

### Reporting Multiple Choice Questions

Most questionnaires use Likert-type scales. Agreement and disagreement categories can be combined to simplify reporting. For example:

> *"Sixty percent (N=18) of the sample either strongly agreed or agreed with the statement that the holiday season tends to cause a great deal of anxiety."*

### Reporting Continuous Data

Continuous data (e.g., months, years, hours) is reported with minimum, maximum, and average values. For example:

> *"Employees reported being involved with the implementation of the New Educational policy for between six months and five years (average = 2 years)."*

### Reporting Open-Ended Questions

Open-ended data is used to support other quantitative data. Responses can be presented as:

1. **Verbatim list** – useful when fewer than 10 responses. De-identify responses by removing any identifying information.
2. **Coded themes** – organize responses into codes or themes, listed from most common to least common.

**Reporting Themes** example:

- Meeting and working with other social activists.
- Seeing positive outcomes due to our work.
- Being involved more in the community.

### Interpreting the Data

**Example 1**

- *Reporting:* Only 26% of the employees interviewed reported knowing how to handle emergency procedures like industrial accidents, fire and earthquake events.
- *Interpreting:* This finding shows how unaware and unprepared employees are in handling emergency situations, and illustrates that efforts to provide emergency training need to be stepped up.

**Example 2**

- *Reporting:* The majority of respondents (75%) said they had to wait for more than an hour before being able to board a bus at Ratna Park in the evening (4 pm–7 pm).
- *Interpreting:* The finding indicates that the frequency of bus service in the evening peak hours is inadequate in Kathmandu.

## Section 5: Language, Typing, Editing and Formatting

### Keep the Language Clear and Straightforward

- Avoid jargon and acronyms, especially those which might be unknown to the general public.
- Use clear and concise writing; include charts and graphs where appropriate.
- Keep sentences and paragraphs short.
- Delete unnecessary words and phrases.
- Use active verbs as much as possible.

### Maintain Objectivity

- Do not use emotionally charged language (e.g., "very" or "extremely") when describing findings.
- Use disappointing results to guide recommendations rather than dismissing or hiding them.
- Discuss limitations in terms of how information was collected, so that audiences can judge the degree of confidence to place in the results.

### Typing and Editing

- The GRP report shall be a typescript paper document.
- Paper size: **ISO A4** white bond paper. If diagrams/tables do not fit, **ISO B4** size may be used (folded and bound to open at the top and right).
- Typing is to be done on **one side of each sheet only**, with pages numbered consecutively.
- Minimum margins:
  - Left: **3.5 cm**
  - Top: **2.5 cm**
  - Bottom: **2 cm**
  - Right: **2 cm**
- Line spacing: **1.5 spaced**. Single spacing may be used only in the Table of Contents, footnotes, endnotes, charts, graphs, tables, quotations, appendices, and references.
- Font: **Times New Roman, size 12** (or equivalent), except for text in tables.
- SOMTU expects a high standard of editing.

### Formatting

- Report writing format should follow **APA** citation and references.
- All text (except text in tables) must always be **justified**.
