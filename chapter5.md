## 5.1 Introduction

Chapter V interprets the statistical results presented in Chapter IV and converts them into analytical, managerial, and policy-level insights. The chapter is structured according to SOMTU GRP guidance for the final chapter: (i) discussion of findings, (ii) conclusions and implications, (iii) recommendations, (iv) limitations, and (v) directions for future research. The aim is not only to restate coefficients, but to explain what those coefficients mean for online shopping behavior, trust formation, and post-purchase outcomes in Nepal.

The study investigated whether payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch increase consumers' risk perception, and whether risk perception influences post-purchase behavior and customer satisfaction. It also examined whether risk perception mediates the relationship between the three antecedent risks and post-purchase behavior. Using 184 valid responses and regression/bootstrapping procedures, all hypothesized core paths were statistically supported. This chapter discusses these findings in relation to the research objectives, the underlying theory, and Nepal's evolving e-commerce context.

The discussion proceeds in three layers. First, each hypothesis is interpreted individually. Second, the results are integrated into a unified behavioral mechanism. Third, implications are elaborated for theory, practice, and regulation. In this way, the chapter demonstrates how the empirical evidence contributes to both academic understanding and actionable improvement in Nepal's digital marketplace ecosystem.

## 5.2 Discussion of Findings

### 5.2.1 Overview of Empirical Pattern

The findings reveal a coherent and internally consistent pattern. Payment issues, scam exposure, and product mismatch all positively and significantly predict risk perception. Risk perception, in turn, increases adverse post-purchase behavior and reduces customer satisfaction. Mediation analysis confirms that risk perception partially transmits the influence of the three antecedent problems to post-purchase outcomes.

This structure indicates that Nepalese online shoppers do not evaluate payment, fraud, and quality signals in isolation. Instead, they cognitively aggregate these signals into a generalized perception of transaction risk, and this perceived risk then shapes behavioral and attitudinal outcomes after purchase. At the same time, the direct effects from antecedent variables to post-purchase behavior remain meaningful in the full model, suggesting that certain harms also produce immediate behavioral consequences outside the mediating pathway.

The pattern is therefore best understood as a dual-channel process: (a) an indirect psychological channel through risk perception and (b) a direct experiential channel through concrete service failures and incidents.

### 5.2.2 Discussion of H1: Payment Issues -> Risk Perception

The study found a significant positive effect of payment issues on risk perception (β = 0.274, p < 0.001). This result supports the proposition that payment reliability functions as a foundational trust signal in online commerce. In Nepal's context, payment friction includes failed transactions, uncertainty about refund timing, hidden charges, and fear of monetary loss. These issues create anticipatory anxiety, especially when consumers perceive weak recourse mechanisms.

The finding is consistent with the trust-risk literature, which treats financial risk as one of the most salient components of online transaction uncertainty. In practical terms, when customers experience failed digital payments or delayed refunds, they revise their belief about the platform's ability and willingness to protect their money. That revised belief elevates overall risk perception beyond the immediate payment event.

This effect is especially relevant in mixed-payment environments where cash-on-delivery (COD) remains an important fallback. COD does not eliminate risk; it redistributes it. Consumers may avoid card or wallet risk, but still face delivery uncertainty, product mismatch, and dispute risk. Thus, payment issues contribute to a broader perception that the transaction environment is unreliable.

From a behavioral standpoint, payment failures are memorable because they directly involve personal financial control. Even when monetary loss is eventually recovered, procedural burden (multiple follow-ups, unclear status, delayed communication) can sustain high perceived risk. This mechanism helps explain why payment reliability is strategically important not only for operations but for long-term trust and retention.

### 5.2.3 Discussion of H2: Online Scams -> Risk Perception

Online scams emerged as the strongest predictor of risk perception (β = 0.366, p < 0.001). This result indicates that fraud salience is the dominant risk amplifier among the antecedents tested. Scam-related concerns likely combine direct exposure, second-hand stories, social media warnings, and public discourse into a persistent narrative of vulnerability.

Unlike routine service errors, scam incidents carry a high symbolic cost: they suggest intentional deception. Consumers interpret intentional harm as a signal that platform governance, seller verification, or complaint resolution may be structurally weak. As a result, scam concerns may elevate not only fraud risk but also perceived product and payment risk, creating spillover effects across the full transaction journey.

In the Nepalese context, where social-network commerce and informal seller channels coexist with formal marketplaces, consumers may face variable enforcement of quality and identity controls. This heterogeneity increases ambiguity about who is accountable when problems occur. Ambiguity is a known driver of perceived risk, and it helps explain the high magnitude of the scam coefficient.

The practical implication is clear: anti-scam systems are not peripheral compliance tools; they are central trust infrastructure. Strong seller verification, suspicious listing detection, secure messaging, and proactive fraud education can reduce risk perception at scale by addressing both actual fraud incidence and perceived platform vulnerability.

### 5.2.4 Discussion of H3: Product Quality Mismatch -> Risk Perception

Product quality mismatch also showed a significant positive relationship with risk perception (β = 0.183, p = 0.0046). Although smaller than the effects of scams and payment issues, the effect remains substantively important because product mismatch is highly visible in post-delivery experience and directly affects expectation confirmation.

Consumers form expectations through product photos, descriptions, reviews, and seller claims. When delivered products diverge from these cues (wrong item, lower quality, inconsistent specifications), users infer that information reliability is weak. This inference generalizes into risk perception for future purchases.

The lower relative coefficient compared with scams may indicate that consumers sometimes treat quality mismatch as a "performance problem" rather than existential danger. Even so, repeated mismatch can accumulate into distrust. Frequent minor disappointments can produce a chronic risk mindset, especially in product categories where authenticity and specification accuracy are critical.

This finding reinforces the argument that listing integrity is a trust mechanism, not just a merchandising function. Platforms that improve image verification, specification standardization, and return transparency may reduce perceived risk by reducing expectation-disconfirmation frequency.

### 5.2.5 Discussion of H4: Risk Perception -> Post-Purchase Behavior

Risk perception significantly increased adverse post-purchase behavior (β = 0.319, p < 0.001). This result confirms that perceived risk is behaviorally active. It does not remain an abstract attitude; it translates into concrete responses such as complaints, returns, negative word-of-mouth, and seller/platform avoidance.

The implication is that post-purchase behavior is shaped by psychological appraisal as much as by objective transaction outcomes. Two consumers facing similar problems may react differently depending on their pre-existing risk interpretation. When risk perception is high, users may adopt defensive behaviors quickly, escalate grievances publicly, and reduce tolerance for service recovery delays.

For platforms, this means that complaint volume and negative advocacy cannot be managed only through reactive customer service. Risk prevention and confidence-building before and during the transaction are equally important. Clear guarantees, transparent communication, and visible safeguards can lower the psychological trigger level for adverse reactions.

This finding also supports mediation logic in consumer behavior theory: cognition (risk appraisal) links antecedent signals to downstream behavior. Therefore, interventions that reduce perceived risk can generate broad behavioral benefits even if some operational frictions remain.

### 5.2.6 Discussion of H8: Risk Perception -> Customer Satisfaction

Risk perception had a significant negative effect on customer satisfaction (β = -0.210, p = 0.0041). The result confirms that satisfaction in online shopping is not driven only by product utility and convenience; it is also sensitive to perceived safety and control.

When users anticipate possible fraud, payment loss, or unresolved disputes, emotional assurance declines. This emotional burden reduces the perceived quality of the overall shopping experience even if delivery or product performance is acceptable. In other words, perceived risk imposes a psychological "cost" that lowers net satisfaction.

The effect size is moderate, suggesting that satisfaction remains multi-determined, but the direction is robust and meaningful. For managers, this implies that customer satisfaction metrics should be interpreted alongside trust and risk indicators. A platform may maintain reasonable functional performance while still underperforming on satisfaction because users remain uneasy about transaction security or recourse fairness.

### 5.2.7 Discussion of Mediation Hypotheses H5, H6, H7

The mediation tests showed significant indirect effects for all three antecedents through risk perception:

- H5 (PI -> RP -> PP): indirect effect = 0.054, CI [0.011, 0.107]
- H6 (SC -> RP -> PP): indirect effect = 0.076, CI [0.014, 0.150]
- H7 (PQ -> RP -> PP): indirect effect = 0.037, CI [0.004, 0.088]

All confidence intervals excluded zero, confirming mediation. Because direct effects in the fuller model remained meaningful for some antecedents, mediation is interpreted as partial rather than full.

Partial mediation is theoretically important. It suggests that payment issues, scams, and mismatch influence post-purchase behavior in two ways: indirectly through heightened risk appraisal and directly through immediate dissatisfaction or loss events. This dual pathway explains why purely communication-based trust campaigns may fail if operational failures remain unresolved.

Among the indirect paths, scams showed the largest mediated effect through risk perception, aligning with the strong H2 coefficient. This indicates that anti-fraud interventions can produce multiplier effects: reducing scam salience may lower risk perception, which then reduces adverse post-purchase behavior.

### 5.2.8 Integrated Interpretation by Research Objectives

**Objective 1: Identify common payment problems faced by online shoppers in Nepal.**  
Findings confirm that payment-related issues materially increase risk perception. The implication is that payment friction is not a minor transaction inconvenience; it is a trust deficit driver.

**Objective 2: Study how online scams affect customer trust and behavior.**  
Scams are the strongest risk antecedent. Scam salience significantly shapes perceived vulnerability and indirectly drives adverse behavioral responses.

**Objective 3: Understand issues related to product quality mismatch in online shopping.**  
Mismatch significantly elevates risk perception and contributes to negative post-purchase responses. Product information integrity is therefore central to trust formation.

**Objective 4: Examine how these factors affect post-purchase behavior.**  
Risk perception and direct antecedent effects jointly explain adverse post-purchase behavior. Behavioral reactions are both cognitively mediated and incident-driven.

**Objective 5: Determine the relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction.**  
Risk perception significantly reduces satisfaction, indicating that experience quality depends on both functional performance and perceived transaction safety.

The findings therefore address all objectives with statistically consistent evidence and support the study's integrated conceptual model.

## 5.3 Discussion in Relation to Existing Literature

### 5.3.1 Convergence with Prior Theory

The observed relationships converge with trust-risk models in e-commerce research, especially the argument that uncertainty in online transactions requires trust mechanisms beyond standard technology acceptance factors. The findings support the view that risk perception functions as a central explanatory construct linking transaction conditions to behavioral outcomes.

The positive links from payment issues, scams, and product mismatch to risk perception align with established notions of financial risk, security/privacy risk, and performance risk. The positive relationship between risk perception and adverse post-purchase behavior also conforms to consumer coping theories, where perceived threat triggers complaint, withdrawal, or negative advocacy.

The negative effect on satisfaction is consistent with expectation-confirmation logic and broader service quality literature: when users perceive elevated risk, cognitive and emotional assurance decline, reducing overall evaluative satisfaction.

### 5.3.2 Contextual Specificity of Nepal

Although the directional findings align with global literature, the Nepal context adds several distinctive features:

1. **Hybrid commerce structure:** formal marketplaces coexist with social-network sellers, producing uneven governance signals.
2. **Trust-sensitive payment behavior:** digital methods grow, yet COD remains salient due to risk aversion and recourse concerns.
3. **Information asymmetry challenges:** variable listing quality and seller transparency intensify mismatch risk.
4. **Fraud salience in public discourse:** scam awareness appears structurally embedded in consumer narratives.

These contextual factors likely amplify the role of perceived risk and explain why scams emerge as the strongest predictor. Therefore, theories developed in mature digital markets remain relevant, but local market architecture influences effect magnitudes and policy priorities.

### 5.3.3 Areas of Nuance and Extension

The findings extend prior work in three meaningful ways.

First, they integrate multiple antecedent risk dimensions within one empirical model focused on post-purchase outcomes. Many prior studies emphasize pre-purchase intention; this study shows that risk mechanisms continue to operate strongly after transaction completion.

Second, by modeling satisfaction and post-purchase behavior simultaneously, the study demonstrates that risk perception has both behavioral and attitudinal consequences. This supports a broader understanding of e-commerce trust where retention risk and reputation risk coexist.

Third, mediation analysis provides process-level insight rather than only direct associations. The evidence suggests intervention leverage points: reducing antecedent risks can improve outcomes partly by reducing generalized risk perception.

## 5.4 Conclusion

### 5.4.1 Core Conclusion

The central conclusion of this study is that online shopping outcomes in Nepal are shaped by a risk-transmission mechanism. Payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch significantly elevate consumers' perceived risk. Elevated risk then increases adverse post-purchase behavior and reduces customer satisfaction. Risk perception also partially mediates the effects of all three antecedents on post-purchase behavior.

Therefore, the challenge facing Nepal's e-commerce ecosystem is not only operational quality but confidence architecture. Consumers respond not just to what goes wrong, but to what they believe could go wrong. This belief system materially influences complaints, returns, avoidance, and satisfaction.

### 5.4.2 Practical Conclusion

For platforms and sellers, trust cannot be treated as a marketing slogan. It must be produced through measurable system performance: reliable payment completion, strong anti-fraud controls, accurate listings, predictable return handling, and transparent dispute pathways. Where these controls are weak or inconsistently visible, perceived risk remains high and post-purchase outcomes deteriorate.

### 5.4.3 Policy Conclusion

For regulators, consumer protection institutions, and payment ecosystem actors, the findings indicate a need for coordinated governance. Fragmented responses to fraud, refund disputes, and seller misconduct allow risk narratives to persist. Unified standards, reporting mechanisms, and enforceable redress procedures are necessary for sustained trust growth in digital commerce.

## 5.5 Implications of the Study

### 5.5.1 Theoretical Implications

This study contributes to theory in five major ways.

1. **Validation of integrated risk model in Nepal:** The study empirically validates a multi-antecedent risk model in a developing e-commerce context.
2. **Support for risk as a central mediator:** Significant indirect effects confirm risk perception as an active process construct, not a peripheral correlate.
3. **Extension from intention to post-purchase domain:** The model demonstrates that trust-risk dynamics remain powerful after purchase, affecting complaint and satisfaction outcomes.
4. **Dual-outcome framing:** Simultaneous modeling of post-purchase behavior and customer satisfaction provides a richer behavioral architecture.
5. **Contextualized generalization:** The findings show how global trust-risk theory translates into Nepal while preserving local specificity in effect strength and practical priorities.

These contributions support the continued relevance of trust-risk frameworks and indicate opportunities to build more context-aware post-purchase behavior theory for emerging digital economies.

### 5.5.2 Managerial Implications for E-commerce Platforms

For marketplace operators, the results imply that trust management should be treated as a cross-functional strategy. Key implications include:

- **Fraud prevention as strategic priority:** since scams are the strongest risk driver, anti-fraud capabilities should receive top investment attention.
- **Payment reliability as trust KPI:** failed transaction rates and refund lead times are trust outcomes, not only operational metrics.
- **Listing governance as risk control:** product mismatch prevention should be embedded in onboarding, listing audits, and seller performance systems.
- **Prevention over compensation:** reducing incident probability is more effective than relying solely on reactive complaint handling.
- **Visibility of safeguards:** users must clearly see verification badges, buyer protection rules, and dispute status tracking.

Managers should also segment users by risk sensitivity. New users, infrequent buyers, and high-value category shoppers may require stronger reassurance layers than regular low-risk buyers.

### 5.5.3 Implications for Sellers and Merchant Networks

For individual sellers and merchant groups:

- Transparent product representation directly influences platform-level trust.
- Fast and fair complaint response protects reputation and repeat purchase likelihood.
- Proactive communication about dispatch, delays, and replacement options reduces escalation.
- Consistent post-sale service quality can differentiate legitimate sellers in high-risk perception markets.

Seller development programs should include fraud-awareness, listing quality standards, and customer dispute etiquette. This improves both customer outcomes and marketplace sustainability.

### 5.5.4 Implications for Payment Service Providers

Payment providers should interpret the findings as evidence that transaction trust is central to e-commerce growth. Critical actions include:

- minimizing payment failure rates;
- providing immediate transaction status visibility;
- standardizing refund traceability;
- integrating strong fraud detection and alert systems;
- enabling interoperable dispute workflows with marketplaces.

Payment confidence influences consumer willingness to move beyond COD dependence. Therefore, payment providers play a direct role in reducing perceived risk and supporting ecosystem formalization.

### 5.5.5 Policy and Regulatory Implications

Regulatory implications are substantial:

1. **Consumer protection standards** for online marketplaces should define minimum requirements for refunds, dispute windows, and disclosure.
2. **Seller accountability frameworks** should include verifiable identity and enforceable misconduct penalties.
3. **Fraud reporting integration** across cyber agencies, platforms, and payment providers should reduce complaint fragmentation.
4. **Public awareness campaigns** on safe online shopping can reduce scam vulnerability and increase informed platform choice.
5. **Monitoring and disclosure norms** for service reliability indicators can improve accountability and competition quality.

A coordinated policy approach can reduce systemic uncertainty and accelerate trust formation across the digital economy.

## 5.6 Recommendations

### 5.6.1 Strategic Recommendations for E-commerce Platforms

#### A. Strengthen fraud prevention and trust verification

- Implement multi-layer seller verification (ID, bank linkage, address confirmation, periodic revalidation).
- Apply risk scoring for new sellers and high-risk categories.
- Use automated and manual checks to detect suspicious listings and behavior patterns.
- Create rapid fraud response cells with clear escalation authority.

#### B. Improve payment assurance and transparency

- Show real-time payment status and expected confirmation windows.
- Provide standardized refund timers and automatic customer updates.
- Flag delayed refunds and unresolved payment disputes in internal risk dashboards.
- Publish buyer-protection eligibility conditions in plain language.

#### C. Reduce product mismatch systematically

- Enforce stricter listing templates (size/material/specification fields).
- Require evidence-based listing media for selected categories.
- Penalize repeated mismatch incidents using tiered seller sanctions.
- Promote verified-review systems to improve information quality.

#### D. Upgrade complaint and return experience

- Offer in-app end-to-end complaint tracking.
- Commit to service-level timelines for first response and resolution.
- Provide guided resolution options (replacement/refund/partial refund/store credit).
- Measure closure quality, not just closure speed.

#### E. Institutionalize trust governance

- Establish an internal Trust and Safety function with cross-functional authority.
- Track trust indicators in monthly performance reviews.
- Link seller incentives and exposure algorithms to trust-performance metrics.

### 5.6.2 Recommendations for Sellers and Merchant Associations

- Use truthful and complete product descriptions with realistic images.
- Confirm order details proactively before dispatch.
- Communicate delays early and transparently.
- Handle post-sale issues courteously and with documented evidence.
- Join platform-led quality and trust certification initiatives.

Merchant associations can help by creating peer learning forums and minimum service charters that reduce market-wide misconduct.

### 5.6.3 Recommendations for Payment Service Providers

- Improve gateway reliability and reduce false declines.
- Provide instant notifications for failed or reversed transactions.
- Share standardized dispute and refund APIs with marketplaces.
- Expand secure payment options while retaining trust-compatible alternatives.
- Strengthen customer education on payment hygiene and fraud prevention.

### 5.6.4 Recommendations for Regulators and Consumer Protection Agencies

- Develop and enforce baseline e-commerce consumer rights standards.
- Require accessible and time-bound grievance systems for registered platforms.
- Establish integrated digital complaint portals linked to cyber and commerce authorities.
- Publish periodic fraud and dispute statistics for transparency.
- Encourage public-private coordination on anti-scam campaigns.

### 5.6.5 Recommendations for Academic Institutions and Researchers

- Build local evidence on platform trust interventions.
- Promote interdisciplinary research linking marketing, information systems, and policy.
- Develop Nepal-specific validated scales for post-purchase trust outcomes.

## 5.8 Risk Governance Framework and Scenario Interpretation

To deepen practical relevance, this section presents a risk governance framework derived from the study results. The framework translates statistical relationships into coordinated control logic for platforms, sellers, payment providers, and regulators. The objective is to move from isolated corrective actions to system-level trust management.

### 5.8.1 Three-Layer Risk Governance Logic

The findings suggest that effective governance should be designed in three interlinked layers:

1. **Pre-transaction assurance layer**  
   This layer minimizes uncertainty before payment and order confirmation. It includes listing integrity controls, seller verification, fraud warnings, and transparent policy disclosure.
2. **Transaction reliability layer**  
   This layer focuses on payment continuity, status visibility, failure handling, and secure channel control during checkout and authorization.
3. **Post-transaction recovery layer**  
   This layer controls trust damage after incidents through fast complaint intake, traceable dispute resolution, predictable compensation pathways, and fair closure.

The model estimates imply that weakness in any one layer can elevate risk perception, but simultaneous weakness across layers can accelerate trust erosion nonlinearly. Therefore, governance maturity should be evaluated as an integrated profile rather than a checklist of disconnected controls.

### 5.8.2 Trust Erosion and Trust Repair Cycle

The chapter's empirical mechanism can be interpreted as a cyclical process:

- **Trigger phase:** payment friction, scam signals, or mismatch incidents occur;
- **Cognitive phase:** consumers revise risk expectation upward;
- **Behavioral phase:** complaints, returns, public negative sharing, or avoidance increase;
- **Relational phase:** satisfaction and loyalty weaken, increasing acquisition pressure on platforms.

If unresolved, the cycle repeats and broadens through social diffusion (peer stories, public posts, informal warning networks), causing ecosystem-level trust dilution. Conversely, a strong repair cycle can stabilize confidence:

- rapid acknowledgment of incidents;
- transparent case handling;
- fair and timely compensation;
- visible prevention upgrades to reduce recurrence.

This cycle perspective matters because managerial teams often optimize closure rate but ignore narrative repair. In trust-sensitive contexts, narrative repair is essential: customers need evidence that systems improved, not only that one complaint was closed.

### 5.8.3 Consumer Risk Archetypes in Nepalese Online Shopping

Based on the observed pattern and contextual characteristics, platform strategy can benefit from risk-archetype segmentation:

1. **Cautious comparator buyers**  
   These users compare prices and ratings extensively, prefer verified sellers, and are sensitive to weak policy disclosure. They can convert strongly if verification and return assurances are explicit.
2. **Convenience-driven routine buyers**  
   These users prioritize speed and familiarity but react strongly to payment failures. Their retention depends on transaction reliability and frictionless service recovery.
3. **Scam-alert social buyers**  
   These users are highly influenced by social media scam narratives and peer warnings. They require strong anti-fraud visibility and communication reassurance.
4. **Price-first risk-tolerant buyers**  
   These users may accept risk for discounts but still generate high post-purchase volatility when harmed. They need clearer consequence warnings and seller-level reliability cues.
5. **Post-incident defensive buyers**  
   These users previously faced loss or severe dissatisfaction and now exhibit elevated baseline risk perception. Recovery requires personalized reassurance and demonstrably fair outcomes.

Archetype-based design can increase intervention efficiency by aligning trust signals with user sensitivity profiles.

### 5.8.4 Scenario-Based Interpretation of Findings

To support strategic planning, the findings can be interpreted in four plausible platform scenarios.

#### Scenario A: Payment improvement without anti-scam improvement

If payment success rates improve but scam controls remain weak, risk perception may decline modestly but remain structurally high. Because scams are the strongest risk driver, unresolved fraud salience can continue to generate defensive post-purchase behavior.

#### Scenario B: Anti-scam improvement without listing-quality improvement

If fraud incidents decline but product mismatch remains high, users may feel safer from intentional deception but still dissatisfied with reliability of product representation. Returns and complaint traffic may remain elevated.

#### Scenario C: Listing-quality improvement without payment transparency

If product accuracy improves while payment failures and refund opacity persist, risk perception tied to financial control may continue to suppress satisfaction and create avoidance behavior in digitally paid transactions.

#### Scenario D: Integrated improvement across all three antecedent domains

When payment reliability, anti-scam controls, and listing integrity improve simultaneously, risk perception is likely to decline more meaningfully, producing broader gains in complaint reduction, satisfaction recovery, and repeat buying.

These scenarios reinforce the partial mediation result: single-domain interventions can help, but multi-domain integration is required for durable trust gains.

### 5.8.5 Governance Maturity Stages for Platforms

A practical maturity model can guide implementation sequencing:

- **Stage 1: Reactive Compliance**  
  Basic fraud handling and complaint closure exist, but controls are fragmented and mostly incident-driven.
- **Stage 2: Structured Control**  
  Standard operating procedures, service-level timelines, and visible buyer-protection policies are implemented.
- **Stage 3: Predictive Trust Management**  
  Platforms deploy risk scoring, category-level prevention, and early-warning analytics.
- **Stage 4: Ecosystem Collaboration**  
  Platforms share risk intelligence with payment providers and relevant authorities while maintaining user privacy and legal compliance.
- **Stage 5: Trust-Centered Advantage**  
  Trust performance becomes a competitive differentiator tied to brand equity, retention, and long-term profitability.

Most emerging-market platforms operate between Stages 1 and 2. The recommendations in this chapter are designed to support progression toward Stages 3 and 4.

### 5.8.6 KPI Architecture for Executive Decision-Making

To operationalize trust governance, executive dashboards should align leading indicators with lagging outcomes.

**Leading indicators (prevention and process quality):**

- seller verification completion rate by risk tier;
- suspicious listing detection precision and recall;
- percentage of refund cases with proactive status updates;
- time to first risk-warning communication after incident detection.

**Lagging indicators (customer and business outcomes):**

- risk-perception proxy index from post-transaction surveys;
- repeat-purchase rate after prior complaint resolution;
- net negative word-of-mouth ratio in support/social channels;
- category-specific churn after severe dispute events.

A balanced KPI architecture prevents overreliance on a single metric such as ticket closure speed. Closure speed without fairness can reduce superficial backlog while worsening latent dissatisfaction.

### 5.8.7 Communication Strategy as a Risk-Control Instrument

The study suggests that communication quality influences how users interpret risk events. Therefore, communication should be designed as a formal risk-control component:

1. **Before purchase:** clear policy summaries and seller trust signals in plain language.
2. **During transaction:** immediate status updates for payment progress, failure, and reversal.
3. **After incident:** concise explanation of next steps, expected timelines, and accountability points.
4. **After resolution:** confirmation of corrective action and recurrence-prevention commitments.

Communication failure often transforms moderate incidents into high-trust crises. Timely and transparent communication can reduce escalation, improve perceived fairness, and support satisfaction recovery.

### 5.8.8 Equity and Inclusion Considerations

Risk perception and coping capacity may vary by digital literacy, access to payment instruments, and language comfort. Platforms and policy actors should integrate inclusion principles:

- use plain multilingual guidance for core policies;
- provide low-friction support channels for users with limited digital confidence;
- ensure dispute pathways do not require excessive procedural complexity;
- avoid penalty designs that disproportionately burden small legitimate sellers.

Inclusive trust design can improve market participation and reduce confidence gaps across user segments.

### 5.8.9 Institutional Coordination Model

Trust improvement requires coordination across organizational boundaries. A practical coordination model includes:

- **Platform layer:** prevention controls, seller governance, and first-line dispute handling;
- **Payment layer:** secure transaction processing, reversal transparency, and fraud monitoring;
- **Regulatory layer:** enforceable standards, reporting protocols, and consumer rights protection;
- **Public education layer:** awareness campaigns that improve consumer decision quality.

Without coordination, risk may shift from one subsystem to another rather than decline at ecosystem level. Coordination ensures that improvements in one area do not get offset by weaknesses elsewhere.

### 5.8.10 Synthesis of Framework Value

This framework adds applied value by linking statistical evidence to governance design. It explains why anti-fraud tools, payment reliability, listing quality, communication, and policy standards should be managed as one trust architecture. In a high-growth market like Nepal, this integrated approach can reduce post-purchase harm, improve satisfaction stability, and support sustainable digital commerce expansion.

## 5.10 Limitations of the Study

Despite meaningful findings, the study has several limitations that should guide interpretation.

1. **Cross-sectional design:** data were captured at one point in time, limiting causal inference about dynamic trust formation.
2. **Convenience sampling:** although practical, this reduces generalizability across all geographic and socio-economic segments of Nepal.
3. **Self-report bias:** responses may reflect recall limitations, perception distortions, or social desirability tendencies.
4. **Context concentration:** the sample likely reflects more digitally connected users, potentially underrepresenting rural or less connected populations.
5. **Measurement constraints:** some construct diagnostics (e.g., marginal PI reliability; CS item behavior caveat) indicate scope for scale refinement.
6. **Platform heterogeneity not fully modeled:** formal marketplaces and social-commerce channels may differ systematically, but this was not modeled as a full comparative design.
7. **External environment effects:** rapid policy and platform changes in digital commerce may alter risk perception patterns over time.

These limitations do not invalidate the model but require cautious interpretation and motivate stronger future designs.

## 5.11 Suggestions for Future Research

Future studies can build on the present work through several directions.

### 5.11.1 Design and Sampling Improvements

- Use probability-based or stratified sampling across provinces and urban-rural segments.
- Apply longitudinal/panel designs to examine how risk perception evolves after repeated purchase experiences.
- Conduct cohort comparisons between first-time and experienced online shoppers.

### 5.11.2 Model Expansion

- Test moderators such as digital literacy, trust propensity, scam victimization history, age, and platform familiarity.
- Include additional constructs such as perceived justice in dispute resolution, platform transparency, and service recovery quality.
- Examine nonlinear effects and threshold behavior in risk-satisfaction relationships.

### 5.11.3 Comparative and Multi-Group Research

- Compare formal marketplaces with social-commerce channels using multi-group analysis.
- Conduct category-specific studies (electronics, fashion, health products) where risk profiles may differ.
- Compare Nepal findings with neighboring South Asian markets for regional generalization.

### 5.11.4 Mixed-Methods and Qualitative Extensions

- Add qualitative interviews with consumers, sellers, and platform trust teams to explain statistical patterns.
- Analyze complaint narratives and dispute-case text for recurring trust failure themes.
- Use case-study approaches for major fraud or recall incidents to map trust recovery trajectories.

### 5.11.5 Measurement and Analytical Refinement

- Refine and revalidate scale items with stronger psychometric purification.
- Use SEM with larger samples to estimate measurement and structural models simultaneously.
- Test alternative mediation chains and potential reciprocal relationships where theoretically justified.

A sustained research agenda along these lines can produce policy-relevant knowledge and support safer digital commerce growth in Nepal.

## 5.12 Chapter Summary

Chapter V interpreted the empirical findings of the study and situated them within theory and context. The results confirmed that payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch significantly increase risk perception; that risk perception significantly increases adverse post-purchase behavior and decreases customer satisfaction; and that risk perception partially mediates all three antecedent pathways to post-purchase behavior.

The chapter translated these findings into practical, managerial, and policy implications. It proposed detailed recommendations for platforms, sellers, payment providers, regulators, and researchers, and outlined a phased implementation roadmap with measurable indicators. The chapter also acknowledged key study limitations and identified future research priorities.

Overall, the evidence indicates that the long-term health of Nepal's online shopping ecosystem depends on reducing both actual transaction failures and perceived transaction vulnerability.

## 5.13 Final Conclusion

Online shopping in Nepal is advancing rapidly, but trust remains the pivotal condition for sustainable growth. This study shows that when consumers encounter payment uncertainty, fraud signals, and product mismatch, they develop elevated risk perception. That elevated perception drives stronger complaint and avoidance behavior and lowers satisfaction with the online buying experience.

Accordingly, improving digital commerce performance in Nepal requires a trust-centered strategy. Platforms must strengthen fraud prevention, payment assurance, and listing integrity; payment providers must improve reliability and transparency; and regulators must institutionalize fair, visible consumer protection systems. Coordinated progress across these actors can reduce risk perception, improve post-purchase outcomes, and support durable confidence in Nepal's digital marketplace.
