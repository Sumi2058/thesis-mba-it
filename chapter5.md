# CHAPTER V: CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

---

## 5.1 Introduction

This chapter synthesizes the key empirical findings of the study and presents the final conclusions, theoretical and managerial implications, practical recommendations, limitations, and directions for future research. The chapter closes the thesis by linking the statistical evidence from Chapter IV to the broader objective of improving online shopping trust and post-purchase outcomes in Nepal.

---

## 5.2 Summary of Findings

Based on data from 184 valid respondents, the study produced the following major findings:

1. **Payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch all significantly increase risk perception.**
   - The strongest predictor of risk perception was online scams (β = 0.366), followed by payment issues (β = 0.274), and product quality mismatch (β = 0.183).

2. **Risk perception significantly increases adverse post-purchase behavior** (β = 0.319, p < 0.001).

3. **Risk perception significantly reduces customer satisfaction** (β = -0.210, p = 0.0041).

4. **Risk perception partially mediates** the effects of payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch on post-purchase behavior.
   - H5, H6, H7 (mediation of PI, SC, and PQ through RP to PP) were all supported via bootstrapped indirect effects.

5. In the full model of post-purchase behavior, **product quality mismatch and scams remained strong direct predictors**, indicating that not all behavioral effects pass only through risk perception.

6. Demographically, the sample was dominated by 20–30-year-old consumers with Bachelor/Master education, and Daraz/social media were the most used channels.

---

## 5.3 Conclusion

The study concludes that online shopping behavior in Nepal is strongly shaped by a risk-driven mechanism. Consumers who perceive higher payment friction, scam exposure, and product mismatch report higher perceived risk, and this elevated risk contributes to stronger adverse post-purchase responses and lower satisfaction.

The findings support the view that Nepal's e-commerce challenge is not only transactional (delivery/payment quality) but also psychological (risk and trust perception). Therefore, improving platform performance alone is insufficient unless risk perception is actively managed through trust-building systems.

---

## 5.4 Theoretical Implications

This research offers three key theoretical contributions.

First, it empirically validates an integrated model linking payment issues, scams, and product mismatch to post-purchase outcomes through risk perception in a developing-economy context.

Second, it strengthens the applicability of trust-risk e-commerce theory (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008) to Nepal by demonstrating significant risk mediation pathways.

Third, it extends Nepal-focused literature by moving beyond purchase intention and explicitly modeling post-purchase behavior and satisfaction together within one framework.

---

## 5.5 Practical/Managerial Implications

For e-commerce platforms and sellers in Nepal, the evidence suggests:

1. **Scam and fraud management must be prioritized** because scam perception is the strongest risk driver.
2. **Payment reliability and refund responsiveness are strategic trust variables**, not only technical operations.
3. **Product quality assurance and listing authenticity controls** directly affect complaint/return behavior and platform loyalty.
4. **Risk communication** (buyer protection display, transparent grievance channels, visible seller verification) should be treated as part of customer experience design.

For digital payment providers and marketplaces, operational trust indicators (failed transaction rates, refund turnaround, dispute closure rates) should be monitored as key performance metrics.

---

## 5.6 Recommendations

### 5.6.1 Recommendations for E-commerce Platforms

1. Implement stronger seller onboarding and periodic re-verification.
2. Introduce stricter product listing standards (photo validation, specification proofing).
3. Make refund policy timelines explicit and enforceable.
4. Display fraud alerts and anti-scam education in checkout flow.
5. Provide visible post-purchase complaint tracking dashboards.

### 5.6.2 Recommendations for Payment Service Providers

1. Improve transaction reliability and failure recovery.
2. Provide instant failure notifications and traceable refund statuses.
3. Expand secure payment options while preserving COD fallback for trust-sensitive users.
4. Coordinate with marketplaces for unified dispute resolution workflows.

### 5.6.3 Recommendations for Regulators and Consumer Protection Bodies

1. Establish minimum digital marketplace consumer protection standards.
2. Require documented refund/dispute policies for registered online sellers.
3. Strengthen cyber-fraud reporting integration between consumers, platforms, and the cyber bureau.
4. Run public awareness campaigns on scam detection and safe online payment behavior.

---

## 5.7 Limitations of the Study

Despite meaningful findings, the study has limitations:

1. **Cross-sectional design** limits causal certainty over time.
2. **Convenience sampling** constrains generalizability to all Nepalese online shoppers.
3. **Self-reported responses** may include recall and social-desirability bias.
4. **Measurement constraints** exist for some items (e.g., marginal PI reliability and inverse CS5 loading behavior).
5. The dataset reflects one period and may not capture rapidly changing platform and policy environments.

---

## 5.8 Suggestions for Future Research

Future studies should:

1. Use probability or stratified sampling across multiple provinces of Nepal.
2. Conduct longitudinal or panel studies to track risk-behavior dynamics over time.
3. Compare platform-specific models (Daraz vs. social media commerce vs. niche marketplaces).
4. Test moderating effects such as digital literacy, prior scam victimization, and trust propensity.
5. Refine and validate the customer satisfaction scale with stronger indicator purification.
6. Apply full SEM with larger samples for simultaneous measurement and structural testing.

---

## 5.9 Final Conclusion

Online shopping in Nepal is expanding, but its sustainability depends on reducing perceived risk at both operational and psychological levels. The study demonstrates that payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch are not isolated inconveniences; they are systemic antecedents of risk perception that shape post-purchase actions and satisfaction. Reducing these risks through coordinated managerial and policy interventions is essential for building durable consumer trust in Nepal's digital commerce ecosystem.
