## 2.1 Introduction

This chapter presents a systematic review of the theoretical and empirical literature relevant to the five core constructs examined in this study: payment issues, online scams, product quality mismatch, risk perception, and post-purchase behaviour (including customer satisfaction). The review is organised thematically, tracing the intellectual heritage of each construct from its foundational theoretical origins through its application in international e-commerce research and, where available, in the Nepalese context. The chapter proceeds in eight substantive sections. Sections 2.2 through 2.6 review the literature on the five main constructs individually. Section 2.7 synthesises the reviewed literature to identify persistent gaps. Section 2.8 presents the theoretical underpinnings of the study, drawing on the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), the Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA), the Expectation–Confirmation Model (ECM), and the multi-dimensional framework of perceived risk. Section 2.9 presents the conceptual framework that integrates all five constructs within a single mediational model, and explains the measurement of each variable. The chapter concludes by delineating the specific contributions that the present study makes to the existing body of knowledge.

## 2.2 Online Shopping and E-Commerce: Conceptual Overview

Electronic commerce, broadly defined as the buying and selling of goods and services through electronic platforms and web-based technologies (Nguyen et al., 2025), has been the subject of sustained academic inquiry since the mid-1990s. The field's theoretical development has been shaped by contributions from information systems, marketing, consumer behaviour, and organisational psychology, and the volume of published research has grown in close parallel with the growth of the sector itself.

Pavlou (2003) provided one of the most influential early theoretical accounts of e-commerce consumer behaviour by integrating trust and perceived risk into the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM). Pavlou argued that while TAM's constructs of perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use are central to technology adoption, they are insufficient to explain consumer acceptance of online transactions, which are characterised by an inherent uncertainty arising from the spatial and temporal separation of buyer and seller. Trust and risk perceptions were proposed as necessary additions to TAM to account for this uncertainty, and empirical testing across two samples confirmed that both constructs significantly influenced consumers' intentions to transact online.

Kim et al. (2008) extended Pavlou's (2003) framework by developing a more comprehensive trust-based consumer decision-making model for e-commerce. Drawing on data collected from over 450 online consumers using structural equation modelling, Kim et al. demonstrated that trust and perceived risk have strong and independent impacts on e-commerce purchasing decisions. The study identified a range of antecedents of trust—including consumer disposition to trust, the reputation of the online vendor, privacy concerns, security concerns, and the information quality of the website—and showed that trust reduces perceived risk, which in turn increases purchase intention. The model established perceived risk as a pivotal mediating construct in the online purchase decision-making process. These findings have been widely replicated and remain foundational reference points for subsequent e-commerce consumer behaviour research.

In the developing-economy context, Bianchi and Andrews (2012) examined online purchasing behaviour in Chile, one of Latin America's more digitally advanced markets, and found that perceived risk had a significant inverse relationship with consumers' attitudes toward online purchasing, and that attitude in turn had a positive influence on purchase intentions. Importantly, the study found that trust in third-party assurances and a broader cultural environment of trust had the strongest positive effects on online purchase intentions—findings that underscore the importance of contextual factors in e-commerce adoption and that have particular resonance for Nepal, another developing economy with a nascent but rapidly growing e-commerce sector.

Nepal's e-commerce history is brief but instructive. Vaidya (2019) traced the origins of organised retail in Nepal to Muncha House in Ason, and documented the launch of muncha.com in April 2000 as the country's first formal e-commerce venture. The study surveyed 300 Nepalese consumers and found that the primary motivations for online shopping were time-saving, the availability of promotions and offers, the ease of ordering, and access to product information. The dominant problem, however, was the quality of products delivered, followed by wrong deliveries—findings that directly foreshadow the present study's focus on product quality mismatch. Devkota et al. (2021) extended Vaidya's work through a larger survey of 403 Kathmandu Valley residents, finding that 66 percent of respondents believed online shopping was characterised by significant challenges. The most commonly cited challenge was products not matching what was shown on the platform (41.6 percent), followed by poor product quality (32.1 percent), timely delivery issues (10.9 percent), hidden costs (8.75 percent), and cumbersome ordering procedures (6.57 percent).

Chapagain and Aryal (2026) provided the most recent comprehensive mapping of Nepal's e-commerce landscape, documenting the sector's growth drivers—including rapid internet penetration, smartphone proliferation, urbanisation, and the catalysing effect of the COVID-19 pandemic—alongside its structural challenges, which include weak digital payment infrastructure, limited consumer protection regulation, cybersecurity vulnerabilities, and persistent consumer distrust in online transactions. The authors argued that Nepal's e-commerce sector occupies a critical juncture, where sustained growth depends critically on addressing these structural weaknesses and building a safer and more trustworthy online marketplace.

## 2.3 Payment Issues in Online Shopping

Payment-related problems are among the most consistently documented obstacles to e-commerce growth in developing economies. The literature identifies several distinct but interrelated categories of payment issues: gateway failures and transaction errors, limited payment options, insecurity in providing financial information online, hidden or undisclosed charges, and inadequate or absent refund mechanisms.

Pavlou (2003) identified financial risk—the risk of monetary loss through incomplete or distorted information and fraudulent transactions—as one of the two primary dimensions of risk in B2C e-commerce, alongside privacy risk. Pavlou's conceptualisation established the theoretical foundation for understanding payment issues as a source of heightened consumer risk perception, and this connection has been empirically confirmed in multiple subsequent studies. Kim et al. (2008) showed that security concerns, including concerns about the safety of financial transactions, are among the strongest antecedents of consumer distrust in online vendors and thus among the most potent drivers of perceived risk.

In the Nepalese context, payment issues take on a particularly acute character because of the structural underdevelopment of Nepal's digital payment ecosystem. Devkota et al. (2021) documented that hidden costs were among the top challenges identified by Nepalese online shoppers, and noted that the preference for Cash on Delivery (COD)—a payment method that largely bypasses digital payment infrastructure—reflects consumers' distrust of electronic payment systems. Pandey (2025) provided the most direct empirical evidence of the impact of financial risk on online buying behaviour in Nepal. Using a web-based survey of 194 online shoppers in Nepal, Pandey found that financial risk had a statistically significant direct negative effect on online shopping behaviour, while product risk did not reach statistical significance, suggesting that payment security concerns are a more immediately salient deterrent to online shopping in the Nepalese context than product quality concerns alone.

Chapagain and Aryal (2026) elaborated on the specific payment challenges facing Nepal's e-commerce sector, documenting frequent payment gateway failures, limited integration of digital payment platforms, consumer unfamiliarity with card-based and wallet-based payment methods, and inadequate consumer protection in cases of payment disputes. These findings are consistent with broader evidence from South Asian developing economies, where payment infrastructure weaknesses have been identified as among the primary barriers to e-commerce adoption (Bianchi & Andrews, 2012; Kim et al., 2008).

The emerging literature on payment fraud and dark patterns in online retail has further enriched the understanding of payment issues by documenting the ways in which platform design choices can compound payment-related consumer harms. Mathur et al. (2019) conducted an automated analysis of approximately 11,000 shopping websites and identified 1,818 instances of dark patterns—user interface design choices that benefit platforms or sellers by coercing, steering, or deceiving consumers into unintended decisions. Relevant to payment issues, the study documented instances of hidden charges, misleading subscription sign-ups, and false urgency manipulations, all of which can contribute to consumer experiences of financial risk and payment-related dissatisfaction. Luo et al. (2025) further documented the growing sophistication of online payment fraud, noting that fraudulent transactions have become increasingly covert and difficult to detect using conventional approaches, posing serious threats to financial security and consumer trust.

In sum, the literature establishes payment issues as a multi-dimensional construct that encompasses gateway failures, limited options, financial insecurity, hidden charges, and inadequate refund mechanisms, and documents a consistent negative relationship between payment problems and consumer risk perceptions and purchasing behaviour. The specific hypotheses in the present study—H1 (payment issues have a significant positive effect on risk perception) and H5 (risk perception mediates the relationship between payment issues and post-purchase behaviour)—are firmly grounded in this body of evidence.

## 2.4 Online Scams and Consumer Trust

Online fraud and scamming constitute one of the most damaging structural challenges confronting e-commerce ecosystems globally. The academic literature on scams in online shopping encompasses several distinct phenomena: non-delivery fraud (in which consumers pay for goods that are never delivered), counterfeit product fraud (in which substandard or fake products are sold as authentic), phishing attacks (in which fraudsters impersonate legitimate sellers or payment platforms to steal financial credentials), fake seller fraud (in which fraudulent accounts on platforms mimic legitimate businesses), and fraudulent payment gateway manipulation (in which consumers are redirected to fraudulent payment interfaces).

Kim et al. (2008) established that privacy concerns and security concerns are among the most important antecedents of consumer distrust in e-commerce, and that distrust amplifies perceived risk, which in turn suppresses purchase intentions. Scam exposure—whether through direct victimisation or indirect awareness—is a particularly potent activator of all of these antecedents simultaneously, because it simultaneously engages financial risk, privacy risk, and psychological risk perceptions.

Shrestha et al. (2025) provided the most directly relevant empirical evidence from Nepal. Using a mixed-methods approach combining qualitative interviews with a Cyber Bureau official, a fraud victim, and online small business owners, and quantitative survey data from 250 online shoppers in Kathmandu Valley, the study examined how fraudulent experiences and consumer awareness of fraud shaped consumer trust and preference. The findings were nuanced: direct fraudulent experiences did not significantly affect consumer trust or preference, but consumer awareness of online shopping scams showed a significant positive impact on both trust and preference. The authors interpreted this as suggesting that awareness of the possibility of fraud can motivate consumers to exercise greater caution and selectivity, thereby increasing their trust in platforms that are perceived as transparent and secure. However, these findings should be interpreted with caution in light of broader evidence suggesting that scam victimisation does negatively affect consumer behaviour—the null finding may reflect sample characteristics or measurement limitations rather than a genuinely negligible effect of victimisation.

Chapagain and Aryal (2026) documented the cybersecurity dimension of fraud risk in Nepal's e-commerce sector, noting that phishing attacks, data breaches, and fraudulent payment gateways are among the most significant threats to consumers and businesses in Nepal's online marketplace. The study called for a coordinated approach combining technical security measures with consumer education and regulatory enforcement to mitigate these threats.

Nguyen et al. (2025) examined the impact of cyber-fraud perception on e-commerce purchase behaviour in Vietnam, using structural equation modelling to test a mediational model in which perceived behavioural control mediated the relationship between cyber-fraud perception and purchase behaviour. The study found that cyber-fraud perception directly and positively influenced both perceived behavioural control and e-commerce purchase behaviour, and that perceived behavioural control acted as a significant mediator. These findings parallel the present study's mediational model, in which risk perception is expected to mediate the relationship between scam exposure and post-purchase behaviour, and provide cross-cultural validation for the theoretical logic of the present study.

The Navigating Online Shopping Scams study (Shrestha et al., 2025) also highlighted the role of the Nepal Cyber Bureau as the primary institutional mechanism for documenting and addressing e-commerce fraud. The Cyber Bureau's records—showing more than 2,000 cases of online marketplace fraud in 2025 with losses exceeding NPR 31 million—underscore the scale and severity of the problem and its direct relevance to consumer risk perceptions and post-purchase outcomes in Nepal.

The relationship between scam exposure and consumer trust has been theorised in the broader literature as operating through a trust erosion mechanism: each instance of fraud—whether directly experienced or learned about through social networks and media—deposits a small increment of distrust that, over time, compounds into a generalised scepticism toward the entire e-commerce ecosystem (Kim et al., 2008; Bianchi & Andrews, 2012). This trust erosion effect is particularly difficult to reverse because it operates at a psychological level that is not readily addressed by technical countermeasures alone. Rebuilding consumer trust after fraud exposure requires sustained demonstration of transparency, accountability, and effective redress mechanisms by platform operators and regulators. Hypotheses H2 and H6 in the present study—which posit, respectively, that online scams significantly increase risk perception, and that risk perception mediates the relationship between scam exposure and post-purchase behaviour—are grounded in this theoretical and empirical evidence base.

## 2.5 Product Quality Mismatch in Online Shopping

Product quality mismatch—the discrepancy between a product as presented online and as actually received—is perhaps the most extensively documented problem in the online shopping literature, and it is the problem most consistently identified by Nepalese consumers as their primary online shopping challenge (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021).

The theoretical treatment of product quality mismatch in e-commerce draws on Oliver's (1980) Expectation–Confirmation Model (ECM), which proposes that consumer satisfaction is a function of the confirmation or disconfirmation of pre-purchase expectations. When a consumer receives a product that confirms or exceeds their expectations formed on the basis of the platform's product description and images, satisfaction is generated. When the received product falls below expectations—in terms of quality, condition, specifications, or match with the product description—disconfirmation occurs, generating dissatisfaction and triggering post-purchase coping behaviours. In e-commerce, where consumers cannot physically inspect products prior to purchase and must rely entirely on digital representations (photographs, descriptions, dimensions, and reviews), the scope for expectation disconfirmation is inherently greater than in physical retail.

Tanjung et al. (2025) examined the impact of perceived product quality, perceived value, and personality on consumer dissatisfaction and complaint behaviour in the Indonesian e-commerce context, using structural equation modelling with partial least squares (SEM-PLS) and multi-group analysis on a sample of 218 e-commerce consumers. The study found that perceived value was the most significant predictor of dissatisfaction across all consumer complaint types, while perceived product quality did not exert a significant direct effect on dissatisfaction overall, though consumers classified as "Irate" types (those who respond to dissatisfaction by expressing anger to their social circle) showed higher sensitivity to product quality failures. The study identified three consumer complaint behaviour types—Voicers (who complain directly to the seller), Irates (who complain emotionally to others), and Passives (who remain silent)—and found that complaint type moderated the strength of the relationships between antecedents and dissatisfaction. These findings underscore the importance of considering both the nature and direction of product quality problems and the heterogeneity of consumer responses in understanding post-purchase behaviour.

Izumi et al. (cited in Chapter I; 2025) found that product quality failures and wrong deliveries were the most frequent triggers for product returns and negative online reviews among e-commerce consumers, confirming the direct link between product mismatch and specific post-purchase behavioural outcomes. The study's finding that wrong deliveries—a specific type of product mismatch in which the consumer receives an item entirely different from what was ordered—are among the most common causes of negative post-purchase behaviour directly echoes Vaidya's (2019) documentation of wrong deliveries as a leading problem in Nepalese online shopping.

Hipólito et al. (2025) examined the influence of consumer trust, return policies, and risk perception on satisfaction with online shopping experiences, using PLS-SEM on a sample of Portuguese B2C e-commerce consumers. An important finding of this study was that the relationship between risk perception and satisfaction was quadratic rather than purely linear, suggesting that an intermediate level of perceived risk may not substantially reduce satisfaction, but that high levels of perceived risk—such as those that would be generated by severe product quality mismatches—significantly suppress satisfaction. This quadratic relationship offers a more nuanced theoretical account of the risk perception–satisfaction link than prior research assuming a simple linear inverse relationship, and it has implications for hypothesis H8 in the present study, which posits a significant negative relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction.

In the Nepalese context, product quality mismatch has been attributed to several structural factors: weak seller verification and accountability mechanisms on e-commerce platforms, the absence of mandatory product authenticity standards, the difficulty of returning products and obtaining refunds, and the reliance on product photographs and descriptions that are often misleading or inaccurate (Devkota et al., 2021; Vaidya, 2019; Chapagain & Aryal, 2026). Ghimire (2023) noted that negative experiences with product quality in online shopping were among the most important factors shaping Nepalese consumers' subsequent decision-making processes, creating a persistent feedback loop between product disappointment and risk aversion in future transactions.

The hypotheses in the present study that link product quality mismatch to risk perception (H3) and posit risk perception as a mediator between product mismatch and post-purchase behaviour (H7) are thus supported by a convergent body of theoretical and empirical evidence from both international and Nepalese contexts.

## 2.6 Risk Perception in E-Commerce

Risk perception is the cornerstone construct of the present study. In the e-commerce context, risk perception is defined as the consumer's subjective assessment of the likelihood and magnitude of an adverse outcome resulting from an online transaction (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008). This definition emphasises the subjective, cognitive character of the construct: risk perception is not merely the objective probability of a bad outcome, but the consumer's personal assessment of that probability and its potential consequences, shaped by their prior experiences, information environment, and psychological characteristics.

The foundational theoretical treatment of risk perception in consumer behaviour was provided by Cox and Rich (1964) and extended to the online context by a succession of researchers in the late 1990s and 2000s. The dominant conceptualisation in the e-commerce literature treats perceived risk as a multidimensional construct encompassing several distinct but interrelated components. Pavlou (2003) identified two primary dimensions relevant to online transactions: monetary risk (the potential for financial loss) and privacy risk (the potential for unauthorised disclosure of personal information). Kim et al. (2008) proposed a broader taxonomy that includes financial risk, product risk, privacy risk, and security risk. Subsequent scholars have added psychological risk (the anxiety and discomfort associated with uncertain transactions) and performance risk (the possibility that the product will not function as expected) to the construct's dimensionality (Bianchi & Andrews, 2012; Pandey, 2025).

In the present study, risk perception is operationalised as a unidimensional second-order construct that captures the overall level of subjective risk the consumer associates with online shopping in the Nepalese context, encompassing financial, product, privacy, and psychological risk dimensions. This operationalisation is consistent with approaches adopted in comparable studies (Pandey, 2025; Bianchi & Andrews, 2012) and is appropriate for a study that aims to test the mediating role of risk perception as a general construct rather than to decompose its dimensions.

Pavlou (2003) integrated risk perception into the TAM framework and showed that perceived risk had a significant negative influence on transaction intentions across two empirical studies, confirming its role as a key deterrent to e-commerce adoption. Kim et al. (2008) demonstrated that trust and perceived risk are strong and largely independent drivers of e-commerce purchasing decisions, with a trust–risk–intention pathway that has become one of the most extensively tested models in the e-commerce literature. Bianchi and Andrews (2012) demonstrated in a developing-economy context (Chile) that perceived risk had a significant inverse relationship with consumer attitudes toward online purchasing, suggesting that the risk perception–attitude–intention chain is generalisable beyond developed markets.

Pandey (2025) provided the most direct empirical evidence from Nepal, finding that financial risk significantly and negatively affected the online shopping behaviour of 194 Nepalese consumers, while product risk did not reach significance. This finding—that financial risk is the dominant dimension of perceived risk in the Nepalese context—is consistent with the structural realities of Nepal's e-commerce ecosystem documented by Devkota et al. (2021) and Chapagain and Aryal (2026), and reinforces the theoretical importance of payment issues as a driver of risk perception.

Hipólito et al. (2025) introduced an important refinement to the risk perception–satisfaction relationship by demonstrating that it is quadratic rather than linear, with satisfaction initially relatively insensitive to moderate levels of risk but sharply declining at high levels of perceived risk. This finding challenges the conventional assumption of a purely monotonic negative relationship and suggests that platform operators should focus on preventing risk perceptions from reaching high levels rather than on eliminating all risk perception.

Nguyen et al. (2025) contributed a further refinement by demonstrating that cyber-fraud perception—a specific, cognitively salient form of risk perception—directly influences e-commerce purchase behaviour both directly and through the mediating mechanism of perceived behavioural control, suggesting that consumers' sense of their own ability to manage risk moderates the behavioural consequences of risk perception. This finding has implications for understanding how consumer education and empowerment interventions might reduce the negative behavioural consequences of risk perception even when objective risks remain present.

## 2.7 Post-Purchase Behaviour and Customer Satisfaction

Post-purchase behaviour encompasses the full range of consumer responses following the completion of an online transaction. The theoretical foundation for the study of post-purchase behaviour is provided by Oliver's (1980, 1997) Expectation–Confirmation Model (ECM), which proposes that post-purchase satisfaction is determined by the confirmation or disconfirmation of pre-purchase expectations. Satisfaction occurs when perceived performance meets or exceeds expectations; dissatisfaction occurs when perceived performance falls below expectations. Post-purchase dissatisfaction, in turn, motivates a range of coping behaviours including complaint, product return, negative word-of-mouth, and platform-switching. These behaviours collectively constitute post-purchase behaviour as understood in the present study.

Tanjung et al. (2025) identified three primary consumer complaint behaviour types in e-commerce contexts: Voicers, who communicate complaints directly to the seller or platform; Irates, who express dissatisfaction through angry word-of-mouth to their social networks; and Passives, who remain silent even when dissatisfied. The moderation analysis conducted by Tanjung et al. showed that the strength of the relationship between dissatisfaction antecedents (perceived product quality and perceived value) and dissatisfaction outcomes varied significantly across these three complaint types, suggesting that post-purchase behaviour is not homogeneous but is shaped by individual personality traits and situational characteristics.

The e-commerce return behaviour literature has identified several key drivers of product returns in online shopping, including product quality failures, wrong deliveries, damaged goods, and unmet expectations regarding product specifications (Izumi et al., 2025). Returns represent a particularly consequential form of post-purchase behaviour for e-commerce platforms because they are costly to process and, if handled poorly, further erode consumer satisfaction and loyalty. The literature has shown that return policy clarity and generosity can mitigate the negative effects of product dissatisfaction on overall satisfaction (Hipólito et al., 2025), but in Nepal's e-commerce context, return policies are often unclear, poorly enforced, or effectively absent (Devkota et al., 2021).

Negative word-of-mouth (NWOM) and negative online reviews represent a second major category of post-purchase behaviour with important implications for e-commerce platforms. The social transmission of negative shopping experiences through informal networks and review platforms amplifies the individual effects of dissatisfaction, reducing potential new consumers' trust in platforms and sellers and thus creating negative externalities that extend beyond the directly affected consumer. In Nepal, where word-of-mouth communication networks remain highly influential channels of consumer information (Ghimire, 2023), NWOM following payment failures, scam experiences, or product quality disappointments may have disproportionately large negative effects on platform reputation and adoption.

Platform-switching and future purchase intention represent the downstream behavioural consequences of the post-purchase process. Ghimire (2023) documented that prior negative online shopping experiences substantially shaped the decision-making processes of Nepalese online shoppers in subsequent transactions, reinforcing the theoretical proposition that dissatisfaction and elevated risk perception generate a feedback loop that progressively increases consumer risk aversion and reduces future engagement with online shopping. This dynamic is particularly important in the Nepalese context, where consumer trust in e-commerce remains fragile and where negative early experiences may determine long-run adoption patterns.

Customer satisfaction is conceptualised in the present study as the consumer's overall evaluation of the extent to which an online shopping experience meets or exceeds pre-purchase expectations (Hipólito et al., 2025). Satisfaction is treated as a component of post-purchase behaviour and, simultaneously, as the primary outcome variable that links risk perception to downstream consumer responses. Hipólito et al. (2025) confirmed that consumer trust reduces perceived risk and thereby enhances shopping satisfaction, and that risk perception is a significant negative predictor of online shopping satisfaction, consistent with an extensive prior literature (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008; Bianchi & Andrews, 2012). The present study's hypothesis H8—that risk perception has a significant negative effect on customer satisfaction—replicates and extends this evidence to the Nepalese context.

## 2.8 Gaps in Existing Literature

The review of the literature reveals several important gaps that the present study is designed to address.

**First**, while individual dimensions of online shopping problems—payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch—have been examined in separate studies in the Nepalese context (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021; Pandey, 2025; Shrestha et al., 2025), no single empirical study has integrated all three within a unified research model. The fragmentation of the literature means that the relative importance of these three problem categories as drivers of risk perception and post-purchase behaviour has not been assessed comparatively, nor has their combined effect been modelled.

**Second**, the mediating role of risk perception in the relationship between online shopping problems and post-purchase behaviour has not been empirically tested in the Nepalese context. Pandey (2025) examined the direct effects of financial and product risk on online buying behaviour but did not model payment issues, scams, or product quality mismatch as antecedents of risk perception, nor did he test the mediational pathway. The present study fills this gap by constructing and testing a full mediational model.

**Third**, existing Nepalese e-commerce research has focused primarily on purchase intentions and buying behaviour as outcome variables (Pandey, 2025; Ghimire, 2023) rather than on post-purchase behaviour specifically. The post-purchase phase—encompassing complaint behaviour, product returns, negative word-of-mouth, platform-switching, and customer satisfaction—has received comparatively little dedicated attention in the Nepalese context despite its fundamental importance for platform retention and the long-run sustainability of the e-commerce sector.

**Fourth**, the scam literature in Nepal is relatively nascent (Shrestha et al., 2025; Chapagain & Aryal, 2026) and has not been systematically integrated with the risk perception and post-purchase behaviour literature. The present study addresses this gap by including scam exposure as an independent variable in an integrated model alongside payment issues and product quality mismatch.

**Fifth**, the theoretical frameworks employed in prior Nepalese e-commerce studies have not consistently drawn on the full range of relevant theoretical foundations. The present study integrates insights from TAM, TRA, ECM, and the multi-dimensional risk perception framework to provide a more comprehensive and theoretically grounded account of the consumer experience in Nepal's online marketplace.

## 2.9 Theoretical Framework

### 2.9.1 Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)

The Technology Acceptance Model, originally proposed by Davis (1989) and drawing on Fishbein and Ajzen's Theory of Reasoned Action, posits that users' acceptance of a new technology is determined by two primary perceptual factors: perceived usefulness (the degree to which using a technology improves the user's task performance) and perceived ease of use (the degree to which using a technology is free of effort). In the e-commerce context, TAM has been widely used to model consumers' adoption of online shopping platforms, and perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use have been consistently confirmed as significant predictors of online purchase intentions (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008).

The present study draws on the TAM framework primarily as context for understanding why consumers adopt online shopping despite the risks involved: consumers accept the costs (risks) of online shopping because they judge the benefits (convenience, choice, price) to be sufficient. However, when risk perceptions become excessively elevated—as a result of payment failures, scam exposures, or product quality disappointments—the cost-benefit calculus shifts against online shopping, reducing adoption and generating negative post-purchase behaviours. Pavlou's (2003) integration of trust and perceived risk into TAM is the specific theoretical contribution that most directly informs the present study's framework.

### 2.9.2 Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA) and Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB)

The Theory of Reasoned Action (Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975) proposes that human behaviour is determined by behavioural intentions, which are in turn shaped by attitudes toward the behaviour and subjective norms (the perceived expectations of significant others). The Theory of Planned Behaviour (Ajzen, 1991) extended TRA by adding a third determinant of intention—perceived behavioural control—to account for situations where behaviour is not entirely volitional. In the e-commerce context, TRA and TPB have been applied to explain consumers' intentions to purchase online, with attitudes being shaped largely by perceived usefulness and perceived risk, and subjective norms being shaped by the shopping experiences and recommendations of peers and family members (Kim et al., 2008).

Nguyen et al. (2025) drew explicitly on TPB to explain the mediating role of perceived behavioural control between cyber-fraud perception and e-commerce purchase behaviour, finding that consumers' confidence in their ability to control the risks of online transactions moderated the negative effects of fraud perceptions on purchasing behaviour. This application of TPB informs the present study's understanding of the psychological mechanisms through which risk perception translates into post-purchase behavioural outcomes, and supports the theoretical rationale for modelling risk perception as a mediating variable rather than an independent predictor.

### 2.9.3 Expectation–Confirmation Model (ECM)

Oliver's (1980, 1997) Expectation–Confirmation Model (ECM) provides the theoretical foundation for understanding post-purchase satisfaction and behaviour. The ECM proposes a three-stage process: (1) consumers form expectations prior to purchase; (2) after receiving and using the product, consumers assess its perceived performance; and (3) the comparison of perceived performance with prior expectations produces either confirmation (when performance meets or exceeds expectations) or disconfirmation (when performance falls short of expectations). Satisfaction is generated by confirmation, and dissatisfaction by disconfirmation. Dissatisfaction in turn motivates post-purchase coping behaviours including complaining, returning products, sharing negative experiences, and modifying future purchase intentions.

In the e-commerce context, the ECM is particularly applicable because the informational basis for pre-purchase expectations is entirely mediated by the digital platform. Product photographs, descriptions, seller ratings, and platform reviews constitute the primary inputs to consumer expectations, and the scope for expectation disconfirmation is consequently much greater than in physical retail, where consumers can directly inspect and assess products. Payment failures, scam exposures, and product quality mismatches all operate as powerful mechanisms of expectation disconfirmation—each representing a specific way in which the actual transaction experience falls below the expectations set by the platform—and thus generate the negative post-purchase behaviours that are the focal outcome of the present study.

### 2.9.4 Multi-Dimensional Risk Perception Framework

The multi-dimensional risk perception framework, synthesised from the work of Pavlou (2003), Kim et al. (2008), Bianchi and Andrews (2012), and Pandey (2025), provides the theoretical scaffolding for the construct of risk perception as operationalised in the present study. According to this framework, consumers' overall perceived risk in e-commerce is a composite of multiple distinct risk dimensions: financial risk (the potential for monetary loss), product risk (the potential for product quality disappointment), privacy risk (the potential for personal data exposure), performance risk (the potential for the product to fail to perform as expected), and psychological risk (the anxiety and discomfort associated with uncertain transactions). Each of the three independent variables in the present study activates a distinct but partially overlapping configuration of these risk dimensions: payment issues primarily activate financial and privacy risk; scams activate financial, privacy, and psychological risk; and product quality mismatch primarily activates product, performance, and psychological risk. The aggregate effect of all three problem categories on overall risk perception constitutes the core mediational mechanism in the present study's model.

## 2.10 Conceptual Framework and Research Model

### 2.10.1 Research Model

Based on the review of the literature and the theoretical frameworks presented above, the present study proposes an integrated conceptual model in which three categories of online shopping problems—payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch—influence consumers' post-purchase behaviour through the mediating mechanism of risk perception. The model also posits a direct negative relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction as a component of post-purchase behaviour. The model is diagrammed conceptually as follows:

- **Payment Issues** → Risk Perception → Post-Purchase Behaviour
- **Online Scams** → Risk Perception → Post-Purchase Behaviour
- **Product Quality Mismatch** → Risk Perception → Post-Purchase Behaviour
- **Risk Perception** → Customer Satisfaction (negative relationship)

This model integrates the TAM's theoretical emphasis on cost-benefit trade-offs in technology adoption (Pavlou, 2003; Kim et al., 2008), the TRA/TPB's account of the attitudinal and normative determinants of behaviour (Ajzen, 1991; Nguyen et al., 2025), the ECM's explanation of how expectation disconfirmation generates post-purchase dissatisfaction (Tanjung et al., 2025), and the multi-dimensional risk perception framework's specification of the dimensions of consumer risk in e-commerce (Pavlou, 2003; Bianchi & Andrews, 2012; Pandey, 2025).

The model positions risk perception as a mediating variable rather than an independent variable or moderator, in keeping with the theoretical logic established by Pavlou (2003), Kim et al. (2008), and Bianchi and Andrews (2012), all of whom demonstrated that risk perception functions as an intermediate psychological mechanism translating problem experiences into attitudinal and behavioural outcomes. The inclusion of all three independent variables within a single model allows the study to assess the relative importance of payment issues, scams, and product quality mismatch as drivers of risk perception, and to determine whether risk perception fully or partially mediates each of the three pathways.

### 2.10.2 Measurement of Variables

**Payment Issues** is measured using a five-item scale adapted from Devkota et al. (2021) and Pandey (2025), covering payment gateway failures, limited payment options, financial insecurity during transactions, hidden charges, and refund difficulties. Items are rated on a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree).

**Online Scams** is measured using a five-item scale adapted from Shrestha et al. (2025) and Nguyen et al. (2025), covering direct experience of online shopping fraud, awareness of fraud prevalence, perception of platform insecurity, distrust of sellers, and fear of losing money to fraudulent transactions. Items are rated on a five-point Likert scale.

**Product Quality Mismatch** is measured using a five-item scale adapted from Vaidya (2019), Devkota et al. (2021), and Tanjung et al. (2025), covering receipt of products not matching platform descriptions, poor product quality, wrong item delivery, damaged goods on arrival, and the difficulty of returning mismatched products. Items are rated on a five-point Likert scale.

**Risk Perception** is measured using a six-item scale adapted from Kim et al. (2008), Pavlou (2003), and Pandey (2025), covering financial risk, product risk, privacy risk, performance risk, and overall perceived riskiness of online shopping. Items are rated on a five-point Likert scale.

**Post-Purchase Behaviour** is measured using a composite scale covering five dimensions: complaint behaviour (adapted from Tanjung et al., 2025), product return intention, negative word-of-mouth behaviour, platform-switching behaviour, and reduced future purchase intention. Items are rated on a five-point Likert scale.

**Customer Satisfaction** is measured using a four-item scale adapted from Hipólito et al. (2025) and Pavlou (2003), covering overall satisfaction with the online shopping experience, satisfaction with product quality, satisfaction with the transaction process, and likelihood of repeat purchase. Items are rated on a five-point Likert scale. Customer satisfaction is treated as both a component of the post-purchase behaviour construct and as a separately examined outcome of risk perception in accordance with hypothesis H8.

## 2.11 Summary

This chapter has reviewed the theoretical and empirical literature on online shopping, payment issues, online scams, product quality mismatch, risk perception, post-purchase behaviour, and customer satisfaction, with particular attention to evidence from developing economies and from the Nepalese context. The review has identified five key gaps in the existing literature: the absence of an integrated empirical model incorporating all three problem dimensions; the untested mediating role of risk perception in the Nepalese context; the limited focus on post-purchase behaviour as a distinct outcome; the insufficient integration of scam exposure within the broader risk and behaviour framework; and the incomplete theoretical grounding of prior Nepalese e-commerce studies. The present study addresses all five of these gaps through an integrated mediational model grounded in TAM, TRA/TPB, ECM, and multi-dimensional risk perception theory. The conceptual framework, variable operationalisations, and measurement scales presented in this chapter provide the foundation for the research design and empirical analysis described in the chapters that follow.
