# CHAPTER III: RESEARCH METHODS

---

## 3.1 Introduction

This chapter presents the methodological design employed to investigate the influence of payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch on post-purchase behaviour among online shoppers in Nepal, with risk perception as a mediating variable. The chapter is organised into the following sections: research design, population and study area, sample size and sampling technique, instrumentation, operationalisation of variables, data collection procedure, reliability and validity, and data analysis methods. The chapter concludes with a discussion of ethical considerations observed during the conduct of the study.

The methodological choices made in this study are guided by the nature of the research problem, the theoretical framework developed in Chapter II, and the practical requirements of conducting research in the Nepalese e-commerce context. The study is quantitative in nature and follows a deductive approach, in which hypotheses derived from established theory are tested against empirically collected data. The chapter is written in a manner intended to enable another researcher to replicate the study if required.

---

## 3.2 Research Design

A quantitative, descriptive-correlational research design was adopted for this study. A quantitative approach was deemed most appropriate given the study's objective of measuring the strength and direction of relationships between the specified variables and testing a set of formally stated hypotheses. The structured survey instrument, utilising a seven-point Likert scale, generates numerical data that are amenable to statistical analysis and hypothesis testing in a manner consistent with the positivist epistemological stance underlying the study.

The research design is descriptive in the sense that it seeks to characterise the nature and prevalence of payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch as experienced by Nepalese online shoppers. It is correlational in the sense that it examines the statistical relationships between these independent variables, the mediating variable of risk perception, and the dependent variables of post-purchase behaviour and customer satisfaction. A mediational model is tested, in which risk perception is hypothesised to transmit the effects of the three independent variables onto post-purchase behaviour.

The study employs a cross-sectional design, meaning that data were collected from the sample population at a single point in time. A cross-sectional design is appropriate for the present study because it allows the investigation of multiple variables simultaneously within a limited timeframe and at a manageable cost. The cross-sectional approach is widely used in e-commerce consumer behaviour research (Kim et al., 2008; Hipólito et al., 2025; Pandey, 2025) and provides a snapshot of the consumer experience at the time of the survey. The limitations of this design—primarily the inability to establish definitive causal direction—are acknowledged in Chapter I (Section 1.8) and Chapter V.

The primary data collection method is a structured self-administered questionnaire distributed to adult online shoppers who had made at least one online purchase within the twelve months preceding the survey. Secondary data—drawn from published academic literature, government reports, and industry statistics—were used to contextualise the findings within the broader empirical and theoretical landscape.

---

## 3.3 Population and Study Area

The target population of this study consists of adult online shoppers residing in the Kathmandu Valley, specifically the three districts of Kathmandu, Lalitpur, and Bhaktapur, who had made at least one online purchase within the twelve months preceding the data collection period.

The Kathmandu Valley was selected as the study area for several reasons. First, it is the primary hub of Nepal's e-commerce activity, accounting for the largest share of online transactions in the country by volume and value (Devkota et al., 2021). Second, it hosts the greatest concentration of internet users, smartphone owners, and digital payment infrastructure in Nepal, making it the most relevant area for a study of online shopping behaviour. Third, prior Nepalese e-commerce research has been conducted predominantly in the Kathmandu Valley, allowing for comparability with existing studies (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021; Shrestha et al., 2025; Pandey, 2025). Fourth, the practical constraints of self-administered survey distribution favour a geographically concentrated study area.

The accessible population comprises individuals who were reachable through university campuses, professional networks, commercial centres, and online survey distribution channels within the Kathmandu Valley. No complete and up-to-date sampling frame of all online shoppers in the Kathmandu Valley exists; accordingly, a non-probability sampling approach was adopted (discussed in Section 3.4).

The study specifically excludes individuals who had not engaged in any online purchasing within the past twelve months, individuals below the age of 18, and transactions of a business-to-business (B2B) nature.

## 3.4 Sample Size and Sampling Technique

### 3.4.1 Sample Size

In the absence of a known population frame, the sample size was determined using the formula recommended by Cochran (1977) for unknown or large populations:

$$n = \frac{Z^2 \cdot p \cdot q}{e^2}$$

Where:
- *n* = required sample size
- *Z* = Z-score corresponding to the desired confidence level (1.96 for a 95% confidence level)
- *p* = estimated proportion of the population possessing the characteristic of interest (0.5, assumed to maximise the required sample size)
- *q* = 1 − *p* = 0.5
- *e* = margin of error (0.05 for a precision level of ±5%)

Substituting these values:

$$n = \frac{(1.96)^2 \times 0.5 \times 0.5}{(0.05)^2} = \frac{3.8416 \times 0.25}{0.0025} = \frac{0.9604}{0.0025} = 384.16 \approx 385$$

A minimum sample size of 385 respondents was therefore indicated by the formula. To account for anticipated incomplete or invalid responses, a target of 400 questionnaires was set for distribution. Additionally, the adequacy of the sample size was evaluated against the criteria for structural equation and regression analysis, which generally recommend a minimum of 10 observations per item (Hair et al., 2019). With 42 Likert-scale items across six constructs, this criterion requires a minimum of 420 responses; accordingly, 450 questionnaires were distributed to provide adequate buffer for attrition and incomplete responses.

### 3.4.2 Sampling Technique

Given the absence of a complete sampling frame for online shoppers in the Kathmandu Valley, a non-probability, purposive convenience sampling technique was employed. Convenience sampling was adopted because it is cost-efficient, administratively feasible within the time and resource constraints of the study, and has been widely used in e-commerce consumer behaviour research in the Nepalese context (Vaidya, 2019; Devkota et al., 2021; Ghimire, 2023). Respondents were selected on the basis of accessibility and willingness to participate, subject to meeting the eligibility criteria of being an adult resident of the Kathmandu Valley who had engaged in at least one online purchase within the preceding twelve months.

Questionnaires were distributed across multiple channels to improve the diversity of the sample:

1. **University and college campuses** in the Kathmandu Valley, where student populations represent a key demographic of online shoppers.
2. **Professional and workplace settings**, including offices and commercial establishments in Kathmandu city, to capture working adult and self-employed consumers.
3. **Online distribution** via Google Forms shared through email and social media platforms, to reach respondents who preferred digital completion.
4. **Shopping areas and commercial centres**, where shoppers were approached in person and invited to participate.

The multi-channel distribution approach was employed to reduce the demographic homogeneity that typically results from single-venue convenience sampling, and to include respondents from a broader range of age groups, educational backgrounds, and occupational categories.

The limitations of non-probability sampling—primarily its inability to support strict statistical generalisation to the broader population—are acknowledged. The findings are intended to be analytically generalisable to theory rather than statistically generalisable to the population.

## 3.5 Instrumentation

### 3.5.1 Survey Questionnaire

Data were collected using a structured, self-administered questionnaire developed specifically for this study. The questionnaire was designed in English and composed of two main parts: (1) a demographic section, and (2) a measurement section comprising six construct-specific scales.

The questionnaire was constructed on the basis of a thorough review of validated scales from the existing literature and adapted to the Nepalese online shopping context. Each item was reviewed for face validity and contextual appropriateness by the research supervisor prior to finalisation.

The questionnaire is structured as follows:

**Part 1 – Demographic Information**

Respondents were asked to provide information on age group, gender, education level, occupation, frequency of online shopping, and preferred online shopping platform. These demographic variables were used to describe the characteristics of the sample and to support sub-group analyses where relevant.

**Part 2 – Measurement Scales**

Part 2 contained 42 Likert-scale items measuring six constructs across seven sections (Sections 2 through 7):

| Section | Construct | No. of Items | Code Prefix |
|---------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| 2 | Payment Issues | 7 | PI |
| 3 | Online Scams | 7 | SC |
| 4 | Product Quality Mismatch | 7 | PQ |
| 5 | Risk Perception | 8 | RP |
| 6 | Post-Purchase Behaviour | 7 | PP |
| 7 | Customer Satisfaction | 6 | CS |
| **Total** | | **42** | |

All items were measured on a seven-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (*Strongly Disagree*) to 7 (*Strongly Agree*). A seven-point scale was selected over a five-point scale to provide greater discriminatory precision and to reduce central-tendency bias, consistent with recommendations in the psychometric literature and with the practice of prior e-commerce studies employing the same constructs (Kim et al., 2008; Hipólito et al., 2025).

### 3.5.2 Sources and Development of Scale Items

Each construct scale was developed by adapting and contextualising items from validated instruments in the existing literature. The item sources are described below.

#### Payment Issues (PI1–PI7)

The payment issues scale captures consumers' experiences with and perceptions of problems encountered during the payment stage of online transactions in Nepal. Items were adapted from the following sources: Vaidya (2019), who identified payment-related barriers as a primary challenge for Nepalese online shoppers; Pandey (2025), who empirically established that financial risk arising from unreliable payment systems negatively affects online shopping behaviour in Nepal; Devkota et al. (2021), who documented hidden costs and unsafe payment systems as among the top challenges reported by Nepalese online shoppers; Pavlou (2003), who theorised that perceived monetary risk arising from incomplete or distorted information during online transactions undermines e-commerce acceptance; and KC and KC (2024), who highlighted structural weaknesses in Nepal's digital payment infrastructure. The seven items measure payment problems experienced (PI1), system reliability (PI2), payment failure frequency (PI3), refund timeliness (PI4), payment security (PI5), hidden charges (PI6), and availability of payment options (PI7).

#### Online Scams (SC1–SC7)

The online scams scale assesses consumer awareness of, exposure to, and concern about fraudulent activities encountered in Nepalese e-commerce. Items were drawn from and adapted based on Shrestha et al. (2025), who studied the effects of fraudulent experiences and consumer awareness on trust and preference among Nepalese online shoppers; Kim et al. (2008), who demonstrated that security concerns and trust are critical antecedents of online purchase decisions; and Chapagain and Aryal (2026), who documented the prevalence of phishing, fake sellers, and fraudulent payment gateways in Nepal's e-commerce ecosystem. The seven items assess general scam awareness (SC1), worry about being scammed (SC2), perceived prevalence of fake sellers (SC3), personal or vicarious scam experience (SC4), trust erosion due to scams (SC5), seller verification behaviour (SC6), and perceived adequacy of platform protection (SC7).

#### Product Quality Mismatch (PQ1–PQ7)

The product quality mismatch scale measures the extent to which respondents experience or perceive a gap between the quality, specifications, or physical condition of products as presented online and as actually received. Items were adapted from Vaidya (2019), who found product quality to be the single most commonly reported problem in Nepalese online shopping; Devkota et al. (2021), who reported that over 41 percent of Nepalese online shoppers had experienced products not matching what was shown online; Tanjung et al. (2025), who confirmed that product mismatches, damaged goods, and misleading descriptions drive e-commerce consumer dissatisfaction; and Izumi et al. (2025), who identified poor quality and wrong items as the most frequent triggers for product returns. Items cover product–image mismatch (PQ1), lower-than-expected quality (PQ2), misleading descriptions (PQ3), receipt of damaged products (PQ4), difficulty returning products (PQ5), inaccurate seller information (PQ6), and overall product quality dissatisfaction (PQ7).

#### Risk Perception (RP1–RP8)

The risk perception scale assesses respondents' subjective evaluation of the overall riskiness of online shopping, encompassing financial, product, privacy, and psychological risk dimensions. Items were adapted from the foundational work of Pavlou (2003), who proposed that perceived risk—encompassing monetary loss, privacy exposure, and uncertainty—is a core determinant of e-commerce acceptance; Kim et al. (2008), who demonstrated through structural equation modelling that perceived risk has a strong negative impact on e-commerce purchase intentions; Pandey (2025), who empirically established that financial and product risk significantly influence online buying behaviour in Nepal; Hipólito et al. (2025), who confirmed a direct relationship between risk perception and online shopping satisfaction; and Bianchi and Andrews (2012), who found an inverse relationship between perceived risk and consumer attitude in a developing-country context. The eight items capture general riskiness perceptions (RP1), financial risk (RP2), pre-purchase uncertainty (RP3), privacy and data security concerns (RP4), overall transactional uncertainty (RP5), purchase hesitation (RP6), preference for cash on delivery as a risk-reduction strategy (RP7), and the influence of risk on purchasing decisions (RP8).

#### Post-Purchase Behaviour (PP1–PP7)

The post-purchase behaviour scale assesses the range of actions and responses undertaken by consumers following an online shopping experience, particularly in the context of problems or dissatisfaction. Items were adapted from Tanjung et al. (2025), who categorised post-purchase complaint behaviours as voicing, irate, and passive responses and found that perceived value failures drive these behaviours; Izumi et al. (2025), who identified negative reviews and product returns as primary post-purchase outcomes of quality-related dissatisfaction; Hipólito et al. (2025), who confirmed that risk levels shape post-purchase satisfaction and complaint behaviour; and Ghimire (2023), who documented how prior negative online shopping experiences affect future decision-making among Nepalese consumers. Items cover formal complaining behaviour (PP1), product return behaviour (PP2), sharing of negative experiences (PP3), avoidance of problematic sellers (PP4), online review behaviour (PP5), platform-switching behaviour (PP6), and the influence of past problems on future purchase decisions (PP7).

#### Customer Satisfaction (CS1–CS6)

The customer satisfaction scale assesses the overall evaluation of the online shopping experience relative to pre-purchase expectations. Items were adapted from Hipólito et al. (2025), who empirically confirmed that consumer trust and risk perception jointly determine online shopping satisfaction; Pavlou (2003), who established that trust and perceived usefulness positively predict consumer acceptance of and satisfaction with e-commerce; Izumi et al. (2025), who linked the resolution of product quality issues to enhanced satisfaction and loyalty; and Vaidya (2019), who assessed overall satisfaction with online platforms in the Nepalese context. Items measure overall satisfaction (CS1), expectation fulfilment (CS2), satisfaction with services (CS3), continued usage intention (CS4), recommendation intention (CS5), and the effect of perceived risk on satisfaction (CS6).

## 3.6 Operationalisation of Variables

The study employs the following operational definitions and measurement specifications:

| Variable | Role | No. of Items | Scale | Items |
|----------|------|-------------|-------|-------|
| Payment Issues | Independent | 7 | 7-point Likert | PI1–PI7 |
| Online Scams | Independent | 7 | 7-point Likert | SC1–SC7 |
| Product Quality Mismatch | Independent | 7 | 7-point Likert | PQ1–PQ7 |
| Risk Perception | Mediating | 8 | 7-point Likert | RP1–RP8 |
| Post-Purchase Behaviour | Dependent | 7 | 7-point Likert | PP1–PP7 |
| Customer Satisfaction | Dependent | 6 | 7-point Likert | CS1–CS6 |

Composite scores for each construct were computed as the arithmetic mean of the respective items following confirmation of internal consistency. Higher scores on the payment issues, online scams, product quality mismatch, and risk perception scales indicate greater levels of the respective problem or perceived risk. Higher scores on the post-purchase behaviour scale indicate a greater propensity for adverse post-purchase responses (complaining, returning, negative word-of-mouth, platform-switching). Higher scores on the customer satisfaction scale indicate greater overall satisfaction with the online shopping experience.

The mediating variable—risk perception—is specified as intervening between the three independent variables and the dependent variable of post-purchase behaviour. A separate relationship between risk perception and customer satisfaction is also examined, consistent with Hypothesis H8 and Research Objective 6.

## 3.7 Pilot Testing

Prior to the main data collection, a pilot test of the questionnaire was conducted with a convenience sample of 30 online shoppers in the Kathmandu Valley who were not included in the main study sample. The purpose of the pilot test was to assess the clarity and comprehensibility of the questionnaire items, to identify any ambiguous or poorly worded items, to verify the time required to complete the questionnaire, and to obtain preliminary estimates of internal consistency reliability for each scale.

Respondents in the pilot test were asked to complete the questionnaire and to note any items that were unclear, confusing, or difficult to answer. Feedback was collected verbally and through written comments. Minor revisions were made to the wording of two items to improve clarity. The average completion time observed in the pilot test was approximately six minutes, consistent with the estimated time stated in the survey introduction.

Internal consistency reliability was assessed for each scale using Cronbach's alpha. All six scales yielded alpha values above the conventionally accepted threshold of 0.70 (Nunnally, 1978), indicating satisfactory internal consistency at the pilot stage. These values were confirmed in the main data analysis (see Section 3.9).

## 3.8 Data Collection Procedure

Data collection was conducted over a period of approximately eight weeks. Questionnaires were distributed through both face-to-face and online channels as described in Section 3.4.2.

For face-to-face distribution, research assistants approached potential respondents in university campuses, offices, and commercial areas in Kathmandu, Lalitpur, and Bhaktapur. Prospective respondents were briefed on the purpose of the study, assured of the confidentiality of their responses, and invited to participate voluntarily. Completed questionnaires were collected on the spot or within a specified period of up to three days. For online distribution, a Google Forms version of the questionnaire was created and shared via email and social media channels. The online form included the same introduction, confidentiality assurance, and instructions as the paper version.

The eligibility screening criterion—having made at least one online purchase within the preceding twelve months—was confirmed through the first item in the demographic section. Questionnaires submitted by ineligible respondents (i.e., those who had not engaged in online shopping in the past year) were excluded from the dataset during data cleaning.

Completed questionnaires were checked for completeness. Questionnaires with more than 10 percent of Likert-scale items left unanswered, or with evidence of systematic response patterns (e.g., all responses at the same scale point across all items), were excluded from the analysis as potentially invalid.

## 3.9 Reliability and Validity

### 3.9.1 Reliability

Reliability refers to the consistency of a measurement instrument—the degree to which it produces stable and consistent results across repeated administrations or across items measuring the same construct (Nunnally, 1978). Internal consistency reliability was assessed using Cronbach's alpha coefficient for each of the six construct scales. Cronbach's alpha values of 0.70 and above were adopted as the minimum acceptable threshold for internal consistency, consistent with standard psychometric practice (Nunnally, 1978; Hair et al., 2019). An alpha value of 0.80 or above was considered indicative of good internal consistency.

Item-total correlation coefficients were also examined for each scale. Items with corrected item-total correlations below 0.30 were candidates for removal; however, item removal was carried out only if removal would substantially improve Cronbach's alpha without substantively distorting the construct's coverage.

### 3.9.2 Validity

**Content validity** was established through a systematic review of the existing literature to ensure that all theoretically important dimensions of each construct were represented in the item pool. The item sources are documented in detail in Section 3.5.2. The questionnaire was reviewed by the research supervisor for content adequacy prior to finalisation.

**Face validity** was assessed through the pilot test procedure described in Section 3.7, in which respondents were invited to provide feedback on item clarity and appropriateness.

**Construct validity** was evaluated through exploratory factor analysis (EFA) where appropriate, to verify that the items hypothesised to measure each construct load onto a single underlying factor, and through an examination of inter-construct correlations to assess whether theoretically expected patterns of convergent and discriminant validity are observed.

**Convergent validity** was assessed by examining the Average Variance Extracted (AVE) for each construct, with a minimum threshold of 0.50 indicating that the construct explains more variance in its items than does measurement error (Fornell & Larcker, 1981).

**Discriminant validity** was assessed by comparing the square root of each construct's AVE with the inter-construct correlations, following the criterion proposed by Fornell and Larcker (1981): discriminant validity is supported when the square root of a construct's AVE exceeds its correlation with all other constructs in the model.

## 3.10 Data Analysis Methods

The data collected were coded, entered, and analysed using IBM SPSS Statistics (version 26). The following analytical procedures were applied in sequence.

### 3.10.1 Data Cleaning and Preparation

Prior to analysis, the dataset was examined for missing values, outliers, and data entry errors. Missing values were handled using listwise deletion for cases with missing data on key construct items. Multivariate outliers were identified using Mahalanobis distance and examined for their influence on the analysis. Descriptive checks were performed to confirm that responses fell within the permissible range of the seven-point scale.

### 3.10.2 Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics were computed for all demographic variables and all Likert-scale items. Frequencies and percentages were reported for categorical demographic variables. Means and standard deviations were computed for continuous variables and construct scores. Descriptive analysis of construct scores provides a characterisation of the general levels of payment issues, scam exposure, product quality mismatch, risk perception, post-purchase behaviour, and customer satisfaction experienced by the sample.

### 3.10.3 Reliability Analysis

Cronbach's alpha coefficients were computed for each of the six construct scales using SPSS. Item-total correlations were examined, and the effect of item removal on alpha was assessed. The final Cronbach's alpha values reported in Chapter IV reflect the complete retained item sets.

### 3.10.4 Correlation Analysis

Pearson product-moment correlation coefficients were computed to examine the bivariate relationships between construct scores. Correlation analysis provides an initial assessment of the direction and strength of the relationships among the six study variables, prior to the multivariate analyses. Correlations were interpreted using the conventional benchmarks proposed by Cohen (1988): |r| < 0.10 (negligible), 0.10 ≤ |r| < 0.30 (small), 0.30 ≤ |r| < 0.50 (moderate), |r| ≥ 0.50 (large).

### 3.10.5 Multiple Regression Analysis

Multiple regression analysis was employed to examine the direct effects of the three independent variables—payment issues, online scams, and product quality mismatch—on risk perception (Model 1), and the direct effect of risk perception on post-purchase behaviour (Model 2) and on customer satisfaction (Model 3). Regression assumptions—including normality of residuals, homoscedasticity, absence of multicollinearity, and independence of errors—were checked prior to interpretation.

Multicollinearity was assessed using variance inflation factor (VIF) values, with a threshold of VIF > 10 indicating problematic multicollinearity (Hair et al., 2019). The coefficient of determination (R²) was examined to assess the proportion of variance in each dependent variable explained by the predictors.

Standardised regression coefficients (β) and their associated significance levels (p-values) were used to evaluate the individual contribution of each predictor and to test Hypotheses H1, H2, H3, and H4.

### 3.10.6 Mediation Analysis

The mediating role of risk perception in the relationships between each of the three independent variables and post-purchase behaviour (Hypotheses H5, H6, and H7) was tested using the causal steps approach proposed by Baron and Kenny (1986), supplemented by the bootstrapping-based indirect effect estimation procedure implemented in Andrew Hayes' PROCESS macro (Model 4) for SPSS (Hayes, 2018). Bootstrapping with 5,000 resamples and bias-corrected 95% confidence intervals was used to assess the significance of indirect effects.

Mediation was evaluated by examining:
1. The total effect of each independent variable on post-purchase behaviour (path *c*).
2. The effect of each independent variable on risk perception (path *a*).
3. The effect of risk perception on post-purchase behaviour, controlling for the independent variables (path *b*).
4. The direct effect of each independent variable on post-purchase behaviour after controlling for risk perception (path *c'*).

Full mediation was inferred if the direct effect (*c'*) became non-significant following the inclusion of risk perception as a mediator, while the indirect effect (*a × b*) was significant. Partial mediation was inferred if the direct effect (*c'*) remained significant but was reduced in magnitude, with a significant indirect effect.

### 3.10.7 Hypothesis Testing

Each of the eight hypotheses was tested at a significance level of α = 0.05 (two-tailed). Hypotheses H1–H4 and H8 were tested using multiple regression analysis. Hypotheses H5–H7 were tested using mediation analysis as described in Section 3.10.6. The results of hypothesis testing are presented systematically in Chapter IV.

## 3.11 Ethical Considerations

The study was conducted in accordance with the ethical standards applicable to social science research. The following ethical safeguards were observed throughout the study.

**Informed consent:** All respondents were informed of the purpose of the study, the voluntary nature of their participation, and their right to withdraw at any time without consequence, prior to completing the questionnaire. Informed consent was indicated by the respondent's decision to proceed with and submit the questionnaire.

**Confidentiality and anonymity:** Respondents were assured that their individual responses would be kept strictly confidential and that no personally identifiable information would be collected. Questionnaires were not linked to the identities of respondents, and data were stored and analysed in aggregate form. No personally sensitive information (such as income, specific purchase histories, or names) was solicited.

**Voluntary participation:** Participation was entirely voluntary. No incentives were offered for participation, and no penalties were imposed for non-participation. Respondents who chose not to participate or to withdraw were not subjected to any negative consequences.

**Data security:** Data collected through the online survey platform (Google Forms) were stored on password-protected accounts accessible only to the researcher. Paper questionnaires were stored in a secure location. Electronic data files were anonymised prior to analysis.

**Accuracy of reporting:** The study was conducted and reported with academic integrity. Findings are reported honestly, without manipulation or selective omission of results. All sources from which scales, items, or methodological procedures were adapted are fully acknowledged with appropriate citations.

---

## 3.12 Summary

This chapter described the methodological framework employed to examine the relationships between payment issues, online scams, product quality mismatch, risk perception, post-purchase behaviour, and customer satisfaction among online shoppers in the Kathmandu Valley, Nepal. A quantitative, descriptive-correlational, cross-sectional research design was adopted. Data were collected from a target of 450 respondents through a structured self-administered questionnaire using a seven-point Likert scale, distributed via convenience sampling across university campuses, professional workplaces, commercial areas, and online channels. The questionnaire comprised 42 measurement items across six construct scales, each developed by adapting validated items from the established international and Nepalese e-commerce literature. Reliability was assessed using Cronbach's alpha and item-total correlations; validity was evaluated through content, face, and construct validity procedures. Data were analysed using descriptive statistics, reliability analysis, Pearson correlation, multiple regression, and bootstrapped mediation analysis (Hayes PROCESS macro). Ethical safeguards including informed consent, anonymity, confidentiality, voluntary participation, and accurate reporting were maintained throughout the study. Chapter IV presents the results of these analyses.
