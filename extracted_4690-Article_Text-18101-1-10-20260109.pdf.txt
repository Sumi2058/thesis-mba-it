The Impact of Perceived Product Quality, Perceived Value, and 
Personality on Consumer Dissatisfaction and Complaint Behavior Types in 
E-commerce 

Puti Kemala Tanjung, Ujang Sumarwan, Diah Krisnatuti 
School of Business, IPB University, Indonesia 
Email: putikemala@apps.ipb.ac.id, sumarwan@apps.ipb.ac.id, krisnatuti@apps.ipb.ac.id 
Abstract: 
The rise of e-commerce in Indonesia has significantly altered consumer behavior, particularly in post-purchase 
experiences.  Despite  increased  convenience,  online  transactions  often  result  in  dissatisfaction  due  to  service 
failures or product mismatches,  prompting varying complaint behaviors. This study investigates the impact of 
Perceived  Product  Quality,  Perceived  Value,  and  Personality  on  dissatisfaction  levels  in  e-commerce,  and 
explores how complaint type (Voicer, Irate, Passive) moderates these relationships. A cross-sectional survey was 
conducted with 218 e-commerce consumers who had made purchases and submitted complaints, and data were 
analyzed  using  Structural  Equation  Modeling–Partial  Least  Squares  (SEM-PLS)  and  Multi-Group  Analysis 
(MGA).  Findings  show  that  Perceived  Value  emerged  as  the  most  significant  and  consistent  predictor  of 
dissatisfaction across all complaint types, while Perceived Product Quality and Personality showed no significant 
direct effect; however, Irates exhibited higher sensitivity to product quality failures. Complaint type moderated 
the strength of relationships, highlighting behavioral differences in consumer responses. In conclusion, consumer 
dissatisfaction in e-commerce is driven primarily by perceived value rather than product quality, and complaint 
behavior  types  influence  how  dissatisfaction  is  expressed  but  not  necessarily  how  it  is  formed.  The  research 
implies that e-commerce businesses should enhance post-purchase services such as delivery, customer support, 
and  refund  systems,  while  tailoring  complaint  resolution  strategies  based  on  consumer  types  to  reduce 
dissatisfaction and improve retention.

INTRODUCTION 

The rapid advancement of information and communication technologies in Indonesia has 
significantly  reshaped  various  aspects  of  life,  particularly  in  commerce.  One  of  the  most 
impactful  innovations  is  the  emergence  of  e-commerce,  which  enables  online  buying  and 
selling  through  digital  platforms.  The  number  of  e-commerce  users  in  Indonesia  has 
consistently  increased since 2020, reaching  58.63 million  in  2023. Major platforms such as 
Shopee, Tokopedia, and Lazada dominate the market, recording billions of visits (Kemendag, 
2023;  Databoks,  2024).  However,  this  growth  has  also  been  accompanied  by  increasing 
challenges, particularly related to post-purchase dissatisfaction. 

As  the  volume  of  online  transactions  increases,  so  does  the  likelihood  of  consumer 
dissatisfaction,  which  often  manifests  through  complaints.  Common  issues  include  delayed 
delivery,  product  mismatches,  damaged  goods,  and  unresponsive  customer  service.  The 
Indonesian Consumers Foundation (YLKI) recorded 943 complaints in 2023, of which 13.1% 
were related to e-commerce transactions (Indonesia.go.id, 2024). If these complaints are not 
handled  effectively,  they  can  reduce  customer  satisfaction,  erode  loyalty,  and  damage  a 
company’s reputation, potentially leading to legal and financial risks.

This study is grounded in Consumer Complaint Behavior (CCB) Theory developed by 
Singh (1990), which categorizes consumer responses to dissatisfaction into four types: Voicer, 
Irate, Activist, and Passive. Voicers tend to communicate complaints directly to the company 
in hopes of resolution. In contrast, Irates express dissatisfaction emotionally, usually to their 
social  circle  rather  than  the  company.  Activists  escalate  their  grievances  through  public 
platforms  or  third  parties  such  as  consumer  protection  agencies.  Passive  consumers,  on  the 
other hand, remain silent even when dissatisfied. In the context of e-commerce, this diversity 
in  complaint  behavior  presents  a  critical  challenge  for  companies  attempting  to  maintain 
customer relationships and brand integrity. 

The research focuses on identifying the antecedent factors that influence such complaint 
behavior,  namely  Perceived  Product  Quality,  Perceived  Value,  and  Personality.  Perceived 
Product Quality refers to the consumer’s judgment of a product’s overall excellence, reliability, 
and conformity to expectations (Catenazzo & Paulssen, 2023). Negative experiences, such as 
receiving a faulty item or one that does not match its description, are among the leading causes 
of dissatisfaction and complaints. 

Perceived  Value  also  plays  a  vital  role,  particularly  in  its  emotional  and  social 
dimensions.  Emotional  value  involves  consumers’  affective  responses,  such  as  satisfaction, 
pride,  or  disappointment,  while  social  value  pertains  to  the  societal  recognition  or  image 
derived from using the product (Momeñe et al., 2023; Cox et al., 2014). When these values are 
not met—for example, when customer service is unhelpful or the product does not enhance 
social status—consumers may feel compelled to complain or even switch platforms. 

Personality  traits further shape how individuals respond to  dissatisfaction. Consumers 
with  assertive  personalities  are  more  inclined  to  express  dissatisfaction  directly  and 
constructively (Voicers), while passive individuals tend to suppress their reactions, leading to 
silent  churn  or  private  word-of-mouth  (Berry  et  al.,  2014;  Prasetyo  et  al.,  2016).  As  such, 
understanding the role of personality helps to predict not only if but how consumers will react 
to negative experiences. 

At the center of these dynamics is the Dissatisfaction Level, defined as the intensity of 
discontent  a  consumer  feels  when  their  expectations  are  unmet  (Boadi  et  al.,  2017).  This 
variable  serves  as  a  mediator  linking  perceived  quality,  perceived  value,  and  personality  to 
complaint behavior. High levels of dissatisfaction are likely to trigger more vocal or public 
forms of complaint, whereas lower levels might result in passivity or disengagement. 

This  study addresses  a critical  gap between the ideal  and actual  patterns  of consumer 
complaint  behavior  in  Indonesia.  Ideally,  companies  aim  to  channel  dissatisfied  consumers 
toward  the  Voicer  category,  where  direct  communication  allows  for  immediate  service 
recovery. However, evidence shows that many consumers default to Passive, Irate, or Activist 
behaviors, which limit recovery opportunities and can lead to reputational harm (Prasetyo et 
al., 2016; Sánchez-García & Currás-Pérez, 2011). 

Therefore,  this  research  aims  to  examine  how  Perceived  Product  Quality,  Perceived 
Value, and Personality influence the Dissatisfaction Level, and further analyzes how complaint 
type  moderates  the  relationship  between  these  independent  variables  and  consumer 
dissatisfaction. The study’s findings are expected to provide strategic insights for e-commerce

RESEARCH METHODS 
Research Design 

This  study  was  conducted  among  e-commerce  consumers  in  Indonesia,  with  most 
respondents coming from the Jabodetabek area, between February and April 2025. It employed 
a cross-sectional survey design to capture data at a single point in time without considering 
temporal  changes  (Wang  &  Cheng,  2020).  This  design  enabled  the  researcher  to  analyze 
current  perceptions,  personality  traits,  complaint  behaviors,  and  dissatisfaction  levels  of 
consumers. The study also adopted an explanatory approach, aiming to investigate the causal 
relationships  between  Perceived  Product  Quality,  Perceived  Value,  and  Personality  on 
Dissatisfaction Level, while examining the moderating role of Complaint Type. This approach 
was appropriate for uncovering the strength and nature of these relationships and for providing 
empirical validation of the proposed conceptual framework. 
Sampling 

The  study  employed  a  non-probability  purposive  sampling  technique,  selecting 
respondents based on specific criteria to ensure alignment with the research objectives. The 
initial  criteria  targeted  consumers  residing  in  Indonesia  who  had  purchased  through  online 
marketplaces and had experienced dissatisfaction with a product. Further criteria required that 
these consumers had submitted a complaint in response to their dissatisfaction, regardless of 
whether  the  complaint  received  a  reply  or  resolution.  Respondents  were  then  classified 
according to Consumer Complaint Behavior (CCB) Theory, particularly into the categories of 
Voicers, Irates, and Passive. This classification ensured the inclusion of relevant behavioral 
variations related to post-purchase responses. The sample size was determined following Hair 
et al. (2017), who suggest a range of 100–200 respondents for SEM-PLS  analysis to ensure 
statistical accuracy and reliability, particularly for models involving independent, intervening, 
and  dependent  variables.  Consequently,  the  study  targeted  200  respondents,  which  was 
sufficient for generating valid and reliable findings for a moderately complex model. 
Measurement 

The study employed a Likert scale for data collection, using a five-point range from 1 
(strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree) to measure respondents’ perceptions and attitudes. The 
research  consisted  of  five  main  variables  with  a  total  of  20  indicators:  Perceived  Product 
Quality  (6  indicators,  adapted  from  Gligor  et  al.,  2019),  Perceived  Value  (5  indicators, 
including emotional and social value dimensions, adapted from Carvache-Franco et al., (2020), 
Dissatisfaction Level (5 indicators, adapted from Boadi et al., 2017), Personality (3 indicators, 
adapted from Prasetyo et al., 2016; Berry et al., 2018), and Complaint Type (1 indicator based 
on  the  typology  by  Singh,  1990).  Complaint  Type  was  treated  as  a  categorical  moderating 
variable representing three behavioral responses: Voicer, Irate, and Passive. All measurement 
items  were  designed  to  capture  the  cognitive  and  emotional  responses  of  consumers  in  the 
context of e-commerce complaint behavior. 

This  study  categorized  consumer  personality  into  two  general  types:  Assertive  and 
Passive, based on how individuals tend to respond to dissatisfaction in e-commerce settings. 
Assertive consumers are typically confident, expressive, and proactive in addressing problems, 
often choosing to file complaints or seek resolutions. In contrast, passive consumers are more 
likely to avoid confrontation, internalize dissatisfaction, and refrain from taking formal action, 
even when dissatisfied. These personality traits are important to understand, as they influence

To  classify  respondents,  a  forced-choice  questionnaire  was  used,  consisting  of  three

passive statements were labeled as Tends to Passive. This approach enabled a clear distinction 
between personality types, allowing further analysis of how personality influences complaint 
behavior.

Data Collection 

Data for this study were collected using an online questionnaire distributed via Jotform. 
The link was shared through digital platforms such as Instagram, WhatsApp, and TikTok to 
reach  respondents  who  met  specific  criteria.  The  questionnaire  included  several  sections: 
general instructions, screening questions to ensure that respondents had purchased products via 
e-commerce  and  experienced  dissatisfaction,  demographic  information,  classification  of 
complaint behavior (Voicer, Irate, Passive) and personality, as well as statements measuring 
the  research  variables.  Only  respondents  who  had  experienced  dissatisfaction  and  had 
submitted a complaint were eligible to continue. All variables were measured using a five-point 
Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). This self-administered 
survey approach ensured efficient data collection and alignment with the study’s objectives. 

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  
Analysis  

This study employed Structural Equation Modeling–Partial Least Squares (SEM-PLS) 
for data analysis using SmartPLS 4.0, consisting of two main stages: outer model evaluation 
and inner model evaluation (Hair et al., 2017). The outer model tested validity and reliability

through  factor  loadings  (≥0.708),  Average  Variance  Extracted  (AVE  ≥0.50)  for  convergent 
validity, and Composite Reliability (CR ≥0.70) for internal consistency. Discriminant validity 
was confirmed using cross-loadings and the Fornell-Larcker criterion. 

The inner model evaluated the structural relationships between latent variables through 
path coefficients, t-statistics, and p-values obtained via bootstrapping (5,000 subsamples). The 
R² values assessed the predictive power of endogenous constructs, interpreted as substantial 
(≥0.75), moderate (≥0.50), or weak (≥0.25). A key analytical technique applied in this study 
was  Multi-Group  Analysis  (MGA),  used  to  assess  the  moderating  effect  of  complaint  type 
(Voicer, Irate, Passive) by comparing differences in path coefficients across subgroups. 

The use of MGA provided several advantages that aligned with the study’s objectives. It 
allowed  for  a  comparative  analysis  between  different  types  of  complainants,  uncovering 
whether  the  strength  and  direction  of  relationships  (e.g.,  between  perceived  value  and 
dissatisfaction) varied across consumer segments. This approach is particularly relevant given 
the  theoretical  foundation  of  Consumer  Complaint  Behavior  (CCB),  which  assumes 
heterogeneous post-purchase responses. MGA enabled the model to capture these behavioral 
distinctions more accurately, providing deeper insights into how each complaint type uniquely 
interacts with psychological and experiential factors in influencing dissatisfaction. Thus, MGA 
enhanced the explanatory richness and practical relevance of the study’s findings. 

Findings  

The  study  analyzed  data  from  218  respondents.  The  majority  of  them  were  female 
(65,14%), while males made up (34,86%) of the sample. The most dominant age group was 
35–44 years (56,42%), followed by those aged 25–34 years (32,11%) and 17–24 years (6,88%), 
indicating that the majority of respondents were in the productive and tech-savvy age range. 
Geographically, most respondents resided in Jabodetabek (56,88%), then West Java (22,48%) 
especially in urban regions, while other provinces. In terms of educational background, most 
participants held a bachelor’s degree (69,27%) or postgraduate degree (18,81%), suggesting a 
highly  educated  consumer  base.  Professionally,  respondents  were  largely  private-sector 
employees  (42,66%),  unemployed  (14,22%),  and  entrepreneurs  (12,39%).  A  significant 
proportion of them earned above IDR 10.000.000 per month (37,16%), although their monthly 
e-commerce  expenditure  was  typically  lower,  with  53,67%  spending  under  IDR  1.000.000. 
These characteristics indicate that the majority of respondents were young, well-educated, and 
financially  capable,  yet  selective  in  their  online  spending—traits  that  can  influence  their 
expectations and complaint behaviors in the e-commerce.

Based on the result shown in Table 2, Voicer was the most dominant complaint behavior 
across all personality types. The Assertive group was the largest with 89 respondents. Most of 
them chose to express complaints directly. This is consistent with previous findings. Assertive 
individuals are confident and proactive in expressing dissatisfaction (Nasurdin et al., 2020). 
Semi-Assertive individuals also showed similar behavior. A total of 50 out of 75 respondents 
in  this  group  became  Voicer.  However,  some  of  them  also  showed  passive  behavior.  This 
reflects the mixed nature of Semi-Assertive personalities (Berry et al., 2018; Kitapci & Dörtyol, 
2015).  Interestingly,  many  Passive  and  Semi-Passive  individuals  also  preferred  to  file 
complaints  directly.  Even  though  they  are  not  typically  expressive,  they  still  chose  formal 
channels. This pattern reflects the modern trend. Even passive consumers are now willing to 
complain when the process is easy and efficient (Tosun et al., 2021). These findings show one 
important  insight.  All  personality  types  prefer  direct  complaint  channels.  That  is  why 
companies must provide simple and responsive complaint systems, 

Shopee was the most used e-commerce platform. A total of 188 respondents used Shopee. 
Shopee was  also  the most  complained-about  platform  with  105 dissatisfaction reports. This 
supports earlier findings. Popular platforms often receive more complaints due to higher user 
volume  and  expectations  (Pramestiti  &  Hidayat,  2024).  Fashion  and  accessories  were  the 
product categories with the most complaints. A total of 106 respondents reported dissatisfaction 
in  this category. This  shows that fashion items  often create disappointment.  Consumers are 
sensitive to mismatched visuals, size, or texture (Sari et al., 2025; Zulkifli et al., 2024). Post-
purchase service was a major issue. Most complaints were related to delivery, return policy, 
and  customer  service.  These  elements  strongly  influenced  how  consumers  evaluated  the 
service. Studies confirm this. After-sales service has a strong effect on satisfaction and loyalty 
(Wulan et al., 2024). The overall conclusion is clear. High transaction volume and sensitive 
product  types  increase  dissatisfaction  risk.  E-commerce  platforms  must  improve  delivery, 
complaint resolution, and product accuracy. These areas are key to improving e-satisfaction 
and customer retention.

dissatisfaction  is  less  about  the  physical  attributes  of  the  product  and  more  about  the 
surrounding service experience. Consumers may accept minor product imperfections as long 
as the transaction process is smooth, transparent, and responsive. 

The  Irates  group,  while  showing  a  relatively  higher  coefficient,  still  presents  a  non-

expectations  rather  than  the  objective  condition  of  the  product  itself.  In  this  sense,  the 
dissatisfaction is emotionally charged and may involve elements such as perceived deception 
or lack of justice rather than mere product flaws.

The Effect of Perceived Value on Dissatisfaction Level 

The  results  of  hypothesis  testing  confirm  that  Perceived  Value  (PV)  significantly 
influences Dissatisfaction Level (DL) consistently across all models. In the complete model, 
the  relationship  between  PV  and  DL  was  significant,  as  it  was  across  all  complaint  type

These findings suggest that consumers are likely to feel dissatisfied when they perceive 
a mismatch between the benefits received and the costs incurred in terms of time, money, or 
effort, regardless of their personality or complaint behavior. Perceived value, as evaluated in 
this study, includes critical elements such as delivery speed, customer service responsiveness, 
shipping costs, and ease of refunds. The consistent significance across models highlights that 
value-based assessments play a central role in shaping consumer dissatisfaction in e-commerce 
transactions.

et  al.  (1991),  and  Regalado-Pezúa  et  al.  (2023),  who  emphasized  that  perceived  value, 
particularly emotional and functional dimensions, significantly impacts consumer satisfaction 
and post-purchase reactions. In e-commerce environments, where consumers cannot physically 
inspect products, the smoothness of service processes becomes essential. Dissatisfaction arises 
notably  when  services  fail  consumer  expectations,  such  as  through  delays  in  delivery, 
unresponsive  customer  service,  or  cumbersome  return  processes,  even  if  the  product  itself 
meets basic standards.

The Effect of Personality on Dissatisfaction Level 

The  hypothesis  testing  results  indicate  that  Personality  (TK)  does  not  significantly

non-significant across all subgroups: Passive, Voicer, and Irates. Due to these consistently non-

how  consumers  express  dissatisfaction,  but  they  do  not  directly  affect  the  intensity  of 
dissatisfaction.  In  other  words,  personality  type  does  not  significantly  determine  whether 
consumers  feel  disappointed,  but  rather  how  they  behave  after  experiencing  dissatisfaction.

to group variation and limited sample size, this pattern was not statistically confirmed.

Marshall  (2015)  showed  that  individual  traits  such  as  impulsivity  and  self-monitoring 
significantly  affect  complaint  behavior,  reinforcing  that  personality  drives  the  type  of 
complaint response rather than dissatisfaction intensity.

personality  on  dissatisfaction  varies  across  different  types  of  complaint  behavior  (Passive,

relationships differed, indicating that each consumer type processes dissatisfaction differently. 
Across all complaint types, perceived value consistently emerged as the most influential 
factor driving dissatisfaction. Consumers are more likely to feel dissatisfied when they perceive 
a  mismatch  between  what  they  paid  and  what  they  received.  This  mismatch  may  relate  to 
aspects such as delivery, customer service, or overall convenience. However, this effect was 
felt  more  strongly  among  Voicers,  who  are  typically  proactive  and  solution-seeking.  This

Limitations 

This  study, while providing  valuable insights,  has several  limitations. First,  the cross-
sectional design limits the ability to observe changes in dissatisfaction and complaint behavior 
over  time  or  across  various  purchase  experiences.  However,  in  this  study,  48.64%  of 
respondents  were  from  the  fashion  and  accessories  product  category,  which  limits  the 
generalizability  of  the  findings,  as  consumer  expectations  and  reactions  may  differ  in  other 
product categories such as electronics or groceries. Furthermore, consumer classification into 
complaint types was based on self-reported behavior, which may be influenced by recall bias 
or social desirability. The model also did not include other potentially influential variables such 
as brand image,  emotional attachment,  or trust  in  the platform, which could  further explain 
dissatisfaction.  Lastly,  since  the  sample  was  concentrated  in  the  Jabodetabek  area  and  its 
surroundings,  the  findings  may  not  fully  capture  the  diversity  of  online  consumer  behavior 
across different regions in Indonesia with varying logistical conditions and digital access.
